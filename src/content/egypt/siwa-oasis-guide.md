Siwa Oasis sits 740 km west of [Cairo](/egypt/cairo-travel-guide) in the Libyan Desert, 50 km from the Libyan border — one of the most remote inhabited places in North Africa, accessible only by a desert highway that crosses 560 km of empty plateau. The oasis itself is 80 km long, sitting in a depression 18 meters below sea level, fed by hundreds of freshwater springs.

Siwa is worth the journey for three reasons: the Oracle Temple where Alexander the Great received confirmation of his divine status in 331 BC; the otherworldly landscape of salt lakes, sand dunes, and date palm groves that looks unlike anywhere else in [Egypt](/egypt); and the Siwan Amazigh (Berber) culture — a distinct people with their own language, architecture, and traditions separate from Arab Egyptian culture.

The trip requires minimum 2–3 nights to justify the distance.

## Getting There

### Overnight Bus from Cairo
The most affordable option — **West Delta Bus Co.** (and other operators) runs overnight buses from Cairo to Siwa.

- Departure: ~10 PM from Cairo (terminal behind the [Egyptian Museum](/egypt/egyptian-museum-guide))
- Arrival: ~7–8 AM in Siwa
- Duration: 9–10 hours
- Price: ~$15–20 USD one-way

The overnight bus is practical — you arrive in the morning with the full day ahead, and the long journey is mostly during sleep.

### Private Car
Drive from Cairo via the Desert Road to Marsa Matrouh (4.5 hours), then south through the desert to Siwa (3 more hours). Total: ~7.5 hours. Requires confidence driving on desert roads.

### Fly to Marsa Matrouh + Drive
EgyptAir operates seasonal flights from Cairo to Marsa Matrouh (1 hour) — from there, 3 hours by road to Siwa. Fastest option when flights are available.

## What to See in Siwa

### Temple of the Oracle (Temple of Amun)

The most historically significant site in Siwa — a hilltop temple where, in 331 BC, Alexander the Great made a detour from his Egyptian conquest specifically to consult the Oracle.

The Oracle of Amun at Siwa was one of the most respected oracular sites in the ancient Mediterranean world. What the Oracle told Alexander is not recorded in detail, but ancient sources report that he emerged having been confirmed as the son of Amun (the Egyptian equivalent of Zeus) — a declaration that Alexander used to legitimize his claim to be the divine ruler of Egypt and, eventually, the world.

The temple itself is partially ruined — massive limestone blocks, carved walls with hieroglyphic inscriptions, and the inner sanctuary where the Oracle spoke. The hilltop setting and the view of the oasis extending below give it an atmosphere that survives its ruined state.

### Mountain of the Dead (Jabal al-Mawta)

A rocky hill covered with rock-cut tombs from the 26th Dynasty through the Greek period — 800+ tombs in the hillside, some with painted decoration. The oldest tombs date to 3rd millennium BC. The hill itself is named for the dead who crowd it, and the experience of walking among the dozens of open tomb doorways is genuinely evocative.

### The Shali Fortress

The ruins of the medieval mud-brick town at the center of Siwa — built from a local salt-clay construction material (kershif) that dissolved gradually after a 3-day rainstorm in 1926. The remaining towers and walls form the skyline of the central oasis, crumbling beautifully.

### Cleopatra's Spring (Ein Guba)

A circular stone-lined natural pool fed by a freshwater spring — the name comes from local tradition rather than documented history, but the spring itself is genuinely ancient. Men and local boys swim here; women have a separate spring (Ein Zeitun).

### Salt Lakes and Dunes

The shallow salt lakes around the oasis — particularly **Birket Siwa** and **Lake Zeitoun** — have an otherworldly quality in morning and evening light. The reflections and the salt crystallization at the lake edges create photographs that look nothing like standard Egypt imagery.

The **Great Sand Sea** begins at Siwa's western edge — one of the largest sand seas in the world, extending into Libya. The dunes closest to the oasis (accessible by 4WD tour) include the **Dakrur Mountain** area for sunset dune experiences.

## The Siwan People

The Siwans are Amazigh (Berber) — not Arab Egyptian. Their language is Siwi, a Berber dialect distinct from Arabic (though Arabic is widely spoken). Their traditional architecture (kershif mud-brick houses decorated with salt-crystal patterns), their weaving (distinctive silver and bead jewelry), and their social organization (traditionally, women were rarely seen outside the home by non-relatives) set them apart from the rest of Egypt.

The oasis has become more integrated with the Egyptian mainstream since road access improved in the 1980s, but Siwan identity remains strong and visible in the market, the mosque architecture, and the way the oasis organizes itself.

## Where to Stay

**Taziry Ecolodge**: The best-known eco-resort in Siwa — mud-brick construction, salt-lake views, and a pool. Popular with international visitors. Prices: 1,500–3,000 EGP per night.

**Shali Lodge**: Inside the Shali Fortress ruins — intimate, atmospheric, and managed by the Egyptian company that has done the most to preserve Siwan heritage tourism. Prices: 1,200–2,500 EGP.

**Budget guesthouses**: Available from 300–600 EGP per night throughout the oasis.

## Practical Notes

**Minimum stay**: 2 full days/3 nights are required to justify the 9-hour bus journey. A rushed 1-night visit misses the oasis atmosphere that accumulates over days.

**Transport in Siwa**: Bicycles (20–30 EGP per day to rent) and donkey carts are the main transport within the oasis. The oasis is flat and bikeable. 4WD trucks (hired locally with driver) are required for the salt lakes and sand dunes.

**Connectivity**: Siwa has mobile data (patchy 3G/4G) and WiFi at the main lodges. Not a fully connected environment — embrace it.

**Best season**: October–April. Summer temperatures in the desert depression are extreme.

**Alcohol**: Siwa is more conservative than Cairo — alcohol is not widely available. Adjust expectations accordingly.
