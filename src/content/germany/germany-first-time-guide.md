Germany rewards preparation. The transport system is world-class once you understand it, the food is better than its reputation, and the cultural rules — punctuality, recycling, quiet hours — are consistently applied. First-timers who arrive knowing the basics have a much smoother trip than those who discover them by accident.

---

## Getting Around: The German Transport System

Germany has one of the best public transport networks in the world. The key is understanding which system serves which trip:

**Deutsche Bahn (DB)** — the national rail network. ICE (high-speed) and IC trains connect major cities. Book at bahn.de; Sparpreis advance fares can be 60–70% cheaper than same-day tickets. The DB Navigator app handles bookings and real-time updates.

**Deutschlandticket** — €29–49/month unlimited travel on all regional trains (RE, RB), S-Bahns, U-Bahns, trams, and local buses across Germany. Not valid on ICE/IC/EC trains. The best value option for multi-city trips.

**Urban transport** (U-Bahn, S-Bahn, trams): Each city has its own transit authority (MVV in Munich, BVG in Berlin, HVV in Hamburg). Day tickets are the best value for tourists; most cities charge €8–10 for unlimited daily travel.

**Validation**: Some German trains and buses require ticket validation before boarding — stamp your ticket at the machine on the platform. On modern systems with digital tickets, no validation is needed. Fare inspectors do check; fines start at €60.

## Money and Payments

**Currency**: Euro (€). Germany has two ATM networks — Geldautomat (everywhere, free with most international cards) and the orange/yellow CashGroup machines (fee-free for member banks).

**Cash culture**: Germany is more cash-dependent than most Western European countries. Many smaller restaurants, market stalls, and traditional pubs (Kneipen) are cash-only. Always carry €20–50 in cash. Supermarkets, hotels, and modern restaurants accept cards.

**Tipping**: Not mandatory but customary. Round up at cafés. At sit-down restaurants, add 10% or round the bill to a convenient number — tell the server the total you want to pay when they come with the bill (don't leave cash on the table and walk away, which is seen as rude). At beer halls and beer gardens, rounding up the Maß price is standard.

**Supermarkets**: Aldi and Lidl are the discount chains (excellent quality, very cheap). Rewe and Edeka are mid-range with better selection. Supermarkets are closed on Sundays.

## Beer Culture

Germany has 1,300 breweries and the Reinheitsgebot (Beer Purity Law, 1516) — the world's oldest food regulation, requiring beer to be made only from water, barley, hops, and yeast. This matters: German beer tastes different because it contains only these ingredients.

**Beer styles you'll encounter:**
- **Weißbier / Weizenbier**: Wheat beer, cloudy, slightly banana-clove aroma. The Bavarian default. Served in tall 500ml glasses.
- **Helles**: Munich's light lager — pale, soft, slightly malty. Augustiner Helles is the benchmark.
- **Pils**: Northern Germany's standard — crisp, hoppy, clear. Bitburger and Warsteiner are national brands.
- **Dunkel**: Dark lager, mild and malty. Common in Bavaria.
- **Märzen / Oktoberfestbier**: The amber festival beer, only officially served at Oktoberfest.

**Beer garden rules**: You can bring your own food to a beer garden but not your own drink. Identify whether you're at a service table (Bedienung — waiter comes to you) or self-service (Selbstbedienung — order at the counter). Mixing them up causes awkward situations.

**Driving**: Germany has strict drink-drive laws — 0.5 mg/ml blood alcohol (lower than UK or US). The German attitude to drink-driving is zero tolerance in practice.

## Language

German is the official language. English is widely spoken in cities, tourist areas, hotels, and among under-40s. In rural Bavaria and eastern Germany, English proficiency drops.

**Useful phrases:**
- Guten Morgen / Guten Tag / Guten Abend — Good morning / afternoon / evening
- Bitte — Please / You're welcome / Here you go
- Danke (schön) — Thank you (very much)
- Entschuldigung — Excuse me / Sorry
- Ein Bier, bitte — One beer, please
- Die Rechnung, bitte — The bill, please
- Sprechen Sie Englisch? — Do you speak English?

**Google Translate** works well for reading menus and signs. Download the German language pack for offline use.

## Cultural Rules First-Timers Often Get Wrong

**Punctuality**: Germans are serious about time. If you have a reservation, a train connection, or an arranged meeting — be on time. Five minutes late for a dinner reservation is noticeable. Ten minutes is rude.

**Quiet hours (Ruhezeit)**: In residential areas, quiet hours typically run 10 PM–7 AM and all day Sunday. Making significant noise during these hours — playing loud music, drilling — is genuinely antisocial and sometimes legally enforceable.

**Crossing the street**: Do not jaywalk at red lights, especially when children are present. Germans wait for the green pedestrian signal. Crossing on red can draw verbal correction from strangers.

**Recycling**: Germany has an elaborate recycling system. In apartments, bins are color-coded: yellow (packaging), blue (paper), brown (organic), grey/black (residual waste). Bottles have a deposit (Pfand, 8–25 cents) — return them at supermarket machines.

**Sunday closures**: Almost everything non-essential closes on Sunday — supermarkets, shops, most services. Plan ahead for Sundays: stock food on Saturday, check which attractions are open (museums usually are).

**Eye contact when toasting**: When clinking glasses, make direct eye contact. Failing to do so, according to a persistent German belief, brings 7 years of bad luck (the Prost superstition is widely known and often deployed).

## Practical Logistics

**Electricity**: 230V, Type F plugs (two round pins). Same as most of Europe. US/UK devices need adapters.

**SIM card**: German carriers (Telekom, Vodafone, O2) sell prepaid SIMs at airports, supermarkets, and electronics stores from €10–20. EU residents can use their domestic SIM without roaming charges.

**Tap water**: Safe to drink everywhere. Ask for Leitungswasser at restaurants (tap water) — it's free or very cheap. Germans generally drink still water; sparkling (Sprudelwasser) is the default bottled option.

**Pharmacies (Apotheke)**: Marked by a red 'A'. Pharmacists are knowledgeable and can advise on minor ailments. Prescription drugs require a prescription; over-the-counter medications are sold here, not in supermarkets.

**Emergency**: 110 (police), 112 (fire and medical). 112 works from any phone, including without a SIM.

## What Germany Is Best For

**History**: No country in Western Europe has a more turbulent and significant 20th-century history. Berlin, Nuremberg, and the concentration camp memorials are essential for understanding it.

**Architecture**: Medieval walled cities (Rothenburg ob der Tauber, Regensburg), Baroque palaces (Nymphenburg, Sanssouci), Bauhaus (Dessau), and contemporary architecture (Berlin's post-reunification building boom).

**Outdoor activities**: The Alps (Bavaria), the Rhine and Moselle valleys, the Black Forest, and the Baltic coast. Germany has excellent hiking and cycling infrastructure.

**Christmas markets**: December brings the world's best Christmas markets — Nuremberg, Cologne, Dresden, Rothenburg, and dozens of others. The season runs late November to December 24.

**Beer festivals**: Oktoberfest (Munich, September–October) is the world's largest folk festival. Regional beer festivals run throughout the year.
