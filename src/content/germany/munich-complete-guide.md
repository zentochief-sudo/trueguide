Munich is the capital of Bavaria and one of the most livable cities in the world — a city with world-class museums, the largest urban park in Europe, an obsessive beer culture, and the Alps 45 minutes to the south. It's the economic engine of southern Germany and a city that manages to feel both cosmopolitan and deeply rooted in regional identity at the same time.

For most international visitors, Munich is the Oktoberfest city. It's also the German city most worth visiting outside of Oktoberfest — the Deutsches Museum is genuinely one of the world's great museums, the English Garden has a river surfing wave in the middle of the city, and the Altstadt (old town) around Marienplatz is the most intact medieval core of any major German city.

---

## Getting to Munich

**By air**: Munich Airport (MUC) — 40 minutes from the city center by S-Bahn (lines S1 or S8, ~€13). One of the best-connected airports in Central Europe.

**By train**: Munich Hauptbahnhof (main station) is a major European rail hub. Direct ICE trains: Frankfurt (3h), Berlin (4h), Vienna (4h), Zurich (3.5h), Paris (6h). Deutsche Bahn handles domestic routes; international connections via Eurail or direct booking.

**By car**: Munich sits at the intersection of major autobahns. Driving into the city center is not recommended — parking is expensive and the public transport system is excellent.

---

## Getting Around Munich

**MVV (Munich transport network)**: Integrated U-Bahn (metro), S-Bahn (suburban rail), trams, and buses. The inner city (Innenraum) single ticket costs €3.50; a day ticket costs €9. The IsarCard 9-Uhr (€57/month) is worth it for longer stays. All public transport options: mvv-muenchen.de.

**Walking**: Munich's Altstadt is entirely walkable. Marienplatz to the English Garden is 20 minutes on foot.

**Cycling**: Munich has excellent cycling infrastructure. Bike rental from MVG Rad (the city's bike-share), Call a Bike, or independent shops from around €15–20/day.

## Marienplatz and the Altstadt

The central square of Munich, dominated by the **Neues Rathaus** (New Town Hall, 1908) with its famous **Glockenspiel** — a 43-bell carillon with 16 life-size figures that perform daily at 11 AM and noon (and at 5 PM in summer). The performance runs 15 minutes and depicts two stories: a 16th-century joust and a dance celebrating the end of the 1517 plague.

**Frauenkirche**: Munich's symbol — the twin onion-domed towers of the Cathedral of Our Lady (1488) visible from throughout the city. Free entry. The view from the south tower (elevator) shows Munich's low skyline, the Alps on the horizon on clear days, and why Munich still restricts building heights to preserve it.

**Viktualienmarkt**: The daily food market immediately south of Marienplatz — 140 stalls of Bavarian produce, cheese, meat, bread, and flowers, operating since 1807. The beer garden at its center is one of Munich's most atmospheric places for a midday Maß. Open Monday–Saturday.

**Hofbräuhaus**: The most famous beer hall in the world (established 1589, current building 1897) — on Platzl, 5 minutes east of Marienplatz. The ground floor holds 1,300 people in traditional vaulted rooms. A tourist pilgrimage site and a genuine Munich institution simultaneously. Best visited on a weekday midday when the crowd is mixed rather than purely tourist groups.

## English Garden (Englischer Garten)

At 3.7 km² (larger than Central Park), the English Garden is the largest urban park in the world. Laid out in 1789, it stretches from the city center north for nearly 10 km along the Isar River.

**The Eisbach wave**: At the southern end of the English Garden, a permanent standing wave on the Eisbach channel has been surfed since the 1970s — the only urban river surfing spot in the world. The wave runs continuously; surfers queue and each gets one uninterrupted run. Spectator-friendly from the Prinzregentenstraße bridge. Best on weekends when experienced surfers put on a show.

**Chinesischer Turm beer garden**: The Chinese Pagoda beer garden in the center of the park seats 7,000 people — one of Munich's largest and most atmospheric outdoor drinking spaces. Open from late morning; full in good weather. Self-service (buy beer and food from the counters).

**Kleinhesseloher See**: An artificial lake in the park's center where locals rent rowboats in summer. Quieter than the southern end.

**Japanese Tea Garden**: A formal Japanese garden in the southern section — small but precisely maintained, with a teahouse.

## Museums

**Deutsches Museum** ★★★: The world's largest science and technology museum — 73,000 exhibits across 22 floors on an island in the Isar River. Aviation (including a WWII Bf 109), marine navigation, mining (actual underground mine simulation), physics, chemistry, musical instruments, and dozens more departments. A full day is not enough. Entry: €16 adults. Open daily 9 AM–5 PM.

**Alte Pinakothek**: One of the world's great collections of Old Masters — Dürer, Raphael, Rubens, Rembrandt, Leonardo. The permanent collection spans 700 years of European painting. Entry: €7 (Sunday: €1). Closed Monday.

**Neue Pinakothek**: 19th-century art including Impressionism and German Romanticism. Entry: €7 (Sunday: €1). Closed Tuesday.

**Pinakothek der Moderne**: Design, graphic art, architecture, and modern/contemporary art under one roof. The building alone (2002) is worth seeing. Entry: €10 (Sunday: €1). Closed Monday.

**BMW Museum + BMW Welt**: The BMW Museum (€10) chronicles 100+ years of automotive and motorcycle history with landmark vehicles. Adjacent BMW Welt is free — the brand's delivery center and architecture statement by Coop Himmelb(l)au. Combined with a factory tour (book well ahead at bmwgroup-classic.com).

**Nymphenburg Palace**: 2 km west of the center — the summer residence of Bavaria's Wittelsbach rulers, set in 200 hectares of baroque gardens. The palace interior (€8) includes the famous Gallery of Beauties (36 portraits of beautiful women commissioned by King Ludwig I). The gardens are free and worth walking regardless.

## Beer Gardens

Munich invented the beer garden. The city has 40+ of them, and sitting outside with a Maß and a Brezn in the afternoon is one of the city's defining experiences.

**Key rules**: At traditional beer gardens, you can bring your own food but not your own drink. Service tables (Bedienung) include a waiter; self-service tables (Selbstbedienung) require ordering from the counter.

**Top beer gardens:**
- **Augustiner Keller** (Arnulfstraße 52) — the most beloved by locals. 5,000 seats under chestnut trees. Augustiner beer from wooden barrels. Near Hauptbahnhof.
- **Chinesischer Turm** (English Garden) — largest, most touristy, but atmospheric. 7,000 seats.
- **Hirschgarten** (Nymphenburg area) — 8,000 seats, the largest beer garden in the world. Deer enclosure adjacent.
- **Seehaus** (English Garden, Kleinhesseloher See lakeside) — the most scenic. Reservations recommended.

## Where to Eat (Beyond Beer Halls)

**Bavarian cuisine essentials**: Weißwurst (white veal sausage, eaten before noon by tradition, with sweet mustard and a Weißbier), Schweinsbraten (roast pork), Leberkäse (pork loaf), Obatzda (cheese spread with butter and paprika), Semmelknödel (bread dumpling).

**Wirtshaus in der Au**: Traditional Bavarian food at non-tourist prices. Kneipe (pub) atmosphere. €15–30/person.

**Tantris**: Munich's long-standing fine dining landmark — French-influenced, one Michelin star. Historic 1971 design by Fritz Haller. Book 4–6 weeks ahead.

**Markt am Wiener Platz**: Neighborhood market in Haidhausen (east of center) — farmers, cheese, bread, fresh produce. Less touristy than Viktualienmarkt.

**Conviva im Blauen Haus**: Updated Bavarian cuisine with seasonal ingredients, in the English Garden area.

## Day Trips from Munich

**Neuschwanstein Castle** (2 hours by train to Füssen, then bus): The 19th-century fairy-tale castle of King Ludwig II — the inspiration for Disney's Sleeping Beauty Castle. Requires advance tickets (book at hohenschwangau.de months ahead). Crowded in summer; arrive before 9 AM.

**Salzburg, Austria** (1.5 hours by train): Mozart's birthplace, Baroque architecture, and the Sound of Music setting. The Altstadt is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Easy day trip.

**Dachau Memorial Site** (40 minutes by S-Bahn): The first Nazi concentration camp (1933). The memorial and museum are sobering and important. Free entry.

**Alps / Garmisch-Partenkirchen** (1.5 hours by train): The gateway to the German Alps — Zugspitze (Germany's highest peak), skiing in winter, hiking in summer, and the Partnachklamm gorge year-round.

## Practical Notes

**Currency**: Euro (€). Cards accepted nearly everywhere.

**Language**: Bavarian German is the local dialect; standard German is universally understood; English is widely spoken in tourist areas.

**Best season**: **Late June–September** for outdoor culture, beer gardens, and Oktoberfest. **December** for Christmas markets (Christkindlmarkt on Marienplatz is one of Germany's best). Winter (Jan–Feb) is cold and quiet; the Alps are excellent for skiing.

**Safety**: Munich is one of the safest large cities in Europe. Normal city awareness applies.

**Tipping**: Not mandatory but customary — round up or add 10% at sit-down restaurants. At beer halls, rounding up the Maß price is normal.

**Tourist card**: The CityTourCard includes unlimited MVV transport and discounts at 80+ attractions. Worth it if you're museum-heavy.
