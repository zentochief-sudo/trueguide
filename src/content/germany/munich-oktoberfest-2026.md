Oktoberfest is the world's largest folk festival — 6 million visitors, 7 million liters of beer, and 17 days of tents, brass bands, roasted chicken, and carnival rides on the Theresienwiese meadow in central Munich. The 2026 edition runs **September 19 – October 4**, opening on the Saturday closest to September 29 (the traditional closing date) as has been the custom since 1810.

This guide covers everything: how to get a tent reservation, what the beer actually costs, what to wear, how to survive the crowds, and how to make the most of Munich beyond the Wiesn.

---

## Oktoberfest 2026 Dates and Key Facts

- **Opening day**: Saturday, September 19, 2026
- **Closing day**: Sunday, October 4, 2026
- **Duration**: 16 days
- **Location**: Theresienwiese (the Wiesn), Munich — 15 minutes from the city center by U-Bahn
- **Entry**: Free (no entry fee to the festival grounds)
- **Tents**: 17 large beer tents + 21 smaller tents; each operated by a different Munich brewery
- **Official beers**: Only Munich's 6 traditional breweries serve at Oktoberfest — Augustiner, Hofbräu, Paulaner, Hacker-Pschorr, Löwenbräu, Spaten

## The Beer: What It Actually Costs in 2026

Oktoberfest beer (called Wiesn-Bier or Märzen) is served exclusively in 1-liter ceramic mugs called Maß (pronounced "moss"). The special Oktoberfest brew is slightly stronger than regular German beer (typically 6.0–6.4% ABV).

**2026 Maß price**: approximately **€16–18 per liter** (prices increase slightly each year; 2025 averaged €16.10–17.20 depending on tent). Expect the upper end of this range for 2026.

| Item | Approximate 2026 price |
|---|---|
| 1 Maß beer (1 liter) | €16–18 |

*Non-drinkers are welcome — each tent serves non-alcoholic options and traditional food. The atmosphere is the same.*

## Tent Reservations: How to Get a Table

The large Oktoberfest tents seat 5,000–10,000 people each. **Reserved tables require advance booking** directly through each tent's official website — most open reservations in January or February for that year's festival. By June, the best time slots are typically sold out.

**What a reservation gets you**: A table for a minimum of 2 hours (some tents require 4 hours), with a minimum food/drink order per person (typically €50–80 in total consumption). The reservation guarantees a seat — without one, you join the walk-in queue.

**Walk-in strategy**: Tents do have unreserved sections (standing room and some tables released for walk-ins). The best times for walk-in entry:
- **Weekday mornings** (opening at 10 AM, noon on Saturdays) — walk up immediately at opening
- **Rainy days** — cancellations and lower attendance
- **First and last days of the festival** — crowds are lighter than peak weekends

**Tent booking websites** (book directly, not through resellers):
- Augustiner: augustiner-braeu.de
- Hofbräu: hb-oktoberfest.de
- Paulaner: paulaner-oktoberfest.de
- Hacker-Pschorr: hacker-festzelt.de
- Löwenbräu: loewenbraeu-oktoberfest.de
- Spaten: spatenfestzelt.de

**Warning**: Third-party ticket resellers charge 3–5x face value and sometimes sell fraudulent reservations. Book only through official tent websites.

## Which Tent to Choose

Each tent has a different character and loyal following:

**Augustiner-Festhalle** — The most traditional and most beloved by Munich locals. Augustiner is the only brewery that still serves from wooden barrels (gravity-fed, no CO2 pressure). Slightly less touristy than Hofbräu. Hardest to get reservations for.

**Hofbräu-Festzelt** — The most internationally famous tent (Hofbräuhaus reputation). Loudest, most tourist-heavy, non-stop party atmosphere. Where international visitors congregate. If you want to meet people from 50 countries, this is your tent.

**Hacker-Festzelt** ("Heaven of Bavaria") — The ceiling is painted as a blue sky with clouds — the most visually distinctive interior. Known for a slightly younger crowd and strong party energy.

**Paulaner Winzerer Fähndl** — One of the largest tents, with an outdoor beer garden that holds 2,500. Good mix of locals and tourists.

**Käfer's Wiesn-Schänke** — The celebrity tent — more expensive, more exclusive, with proper restaurant service. Reservations required months in advance. Not the full Oktoberfest experience but worth knowing about.

**Ochsenbraterei** (Ox Roaster) — Food-focused. The whole spit-roasted ox is the centrepiece. Better food quality than most tents.

## What to Wear

Wearing traditional Bavarian dress (Tracht) is not required but is enthusiastically embraced — even by tourists. First-timers are welcomed in Tracht; showing up in regular clothes is equally fine.

**Women — Dirndl**: A traditional dress with a bodice, blouse, and apron. Quality ranges from €50 (fast fashion) to €500+ (authentic Bavarian). Key detail: **the apron bow placement signals relationship status** — left side = single, right side = taken, back = widowed, center = unclear. Worn without stockings in warm weather; with thick tights when cold.

**Men — Lederhosen**: Leather shorts (short or knee-length) with suspenders. Paired with a checkered shirt and wool socks. Short Lederhosen (Krachlederne) are the traditional Oktoberfest choice. Quality ranges similarly — €80 to €600+.

**Where to buy in Munich**: Traditional shops (Loden-Frey, Trachten Angermaier) in central Munich stock the full range. Buy by mid-September — selection depletes fast.

**Where to rent**: Several rental shops operate near the Theresienwiese during the festival. Convenient for first-timers.

## Getting to the Theresienwiese

**U-Bahn (recommended)**: Lines U4 and U5 both stop at **Theresienwiese** — exit directly onto the festival grounds. €3.50 single ticket from central Munich. The U-Bahn runs until after 2 AM during Oktoberfest.

**Walking**: From Hauptbahnhof (main train station) it's a 12-minute walk southwest along Bavariaring. Most of the city center is within 20–30 minutes on foot.

**Driving/parking**: Strongly discouraged. Road closures around the Wiesn during festival hours make driving a bad option.

## Surviving the Crowds: Practical Tips

**Peak times to avoid**: Saturday afternoons and Sunday afternoons (peak tourist days). The second weekend (September 26–27) is typically the most crowded of the festival.

**Best times to go**: Weekday mornings (10 AM–1 PM) when tents are filling but not yet at capacity. Monday–Thursday evenings are also calmer than weekends.

**Booking the right hotel**: Book Munich accommodation 6–12 months in advance for Oktoberfest dates. Prices during the festival are 2–4x normal rates. Staying within walking distance of the Wiesn (Ludwigsvorstadt, Isarvorstadt) eliminates U-Bahn queuing. Alternatively, book in towns 30–45 minutes by train (Augsburg, Freising) and commute in.

**Mugs**: It is technically illegal to leave the festival grounds with a Maß mug. Many people try anyway; security has checkpoints. The souvenir mugs sold at shops are the legal option.

**Pacing**: A Maß is 1 full liter of 6.3% beer. Two Maß = 2 liters. People who arrive without food and drink two Maß rapidly have poor outcomes. Eat the Hendl, order the Brezn, alternate with water.

**Safety**: The festival has a well-organized medical presence. The main tent zone and U-Bahn exits have dedicated first aid stations. Lost and found is centrally organized. The festival is generally safe; standard crowd-management awareness applies on peak days.

## Beyond the Tents: What Else Is at Oktoberfest

The Theresienwiese isn't just tents — the surrounding area is a full fairground:

**Rides**: Traditional Ferris wheel, roller coasters, and carnival rides ranging from family-friendly to genuinely alarming. The **Olympia Looping** (a classic steel looping coaster) and the **Sky Fall** free-drop ride are Oktoberfest staples.

**Traditional sideshows**: Log-sawing competitions, traditional Bavarian music performances, and the daily parade.

**The Bavaria Statue**: At the western end of the grounds, the 18-meter bronze Bavaria figure (1850) stands above the Ruhmeshalle. Climbable — the interior leads up to the head for panoramic views.

**Oide Wiesn** ("Old Oktoberfest"): A section of the grounds dedicated to traditional 19th-century Oktoberfest atmosphere — historical rides, folk music, and no amplified pop music. Separate entry ticket (~€5). Worth visiting if you want a contrast to the main tent energy.

## Munich Beyond Oktoberfest

The festival is 16 days. Munich is worth more. The city has one of the best collections of museums in Europe, excellent architecture, and a quality of life that makes it consistently one of the most livable cities in the world.

**See the full [Munich travel guide →](/germany/munich-complete-guide)** for the complete breakdown of what to do before and after the festival.

**Must-see within walking distance of the Wiesn:**
- **Deutsches Museum** — the world's largest science and technology museum, on the Isar river island. Full day.
- **Marienplatz + Neues Rathaus** — the city center square, the famous Glockenspiel (chimes at 11 AM and noon)
- **English Garden (Englischer Garten)** — larger than Central Park, with a river surfing wave at the Eisbach

## Frequently Asked Questions

**When is Oktoberfest 2026?**
September 19 – October 4, 2026. Opening ceremony on September 19 at noon (O'zapft is! — the Mayor taps the first keg).

**Is Oktoberfest free to enter?**
Yes. Entry to the Theresienwiese festival grounds is free. Food and drink inside the tents are paid separately. The Oide Wiesn historical section has a small entry fee (~€5).

**Do I need a reservation for Oktoberfest?**
Not to enter the festival grounds — entry is always free. But to guarantee a seat inside the large tents, a reservation is required. Walk-in seats exist but are limited, especially on weekends. Weekday mornings are the best chance for walk-in seating.

**How much does beer cost at Oktoberfest 2026?**
Approximately €16–18 per Maß (1-liter mug). Prices are set by each tent and confirmed in summer 2026.

**What is the dress code at Oktoberfest?**
No dress code — regular clothes are fine. But wearing a Dirndl (women) or Lederhosen (men) is the tradition and widely embraced even by first-time visitors. It genuinely adds to the experience.

**Can children go to Oktoberfest?**
Yes, during daytime hours. Children under 6 are admitted free. Children must leave the tents by 8 PM. The fairground rides and daytime atmosphere are family-friendly; evenings skew heavily adult.

**How far is Oktoberfest from central Munich?**
15 minutes by U-Bahn (lines U4/U5 to Theresienwiese), or 12 minutes walking from Hauptbahnhof. Extremely accessible.
