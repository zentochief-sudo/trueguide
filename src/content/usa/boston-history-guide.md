Boston is the most historically dense World Cup host city in the United States. The events of the American Revolution — the Boston Massacre (1770), the Boston Tea Party (1773), Paul Revere's Ride (1775), the Battle of Bunker Hill (1775) — happened within 2 miles of each other, and most of the significant sites have been preserved or memorialized. For World Cup visitors without strong prior knowledge of American history, a walk along the Freedom Trail provides context for the revolutionary period that created the United States.

---

## The Freedom Trail

The Freedom Trail is a 2.5-mile marked walking route connecting 16 historical sites in Boston and Charlestown. A red brick line embedded in the sidewalk connects the sites — the easiest self-guided historical walk in the US.

**Starting point**: Boston Common, at the Visitor Information Center near the Park Street T station.

**The 16 sites**:

**Boston Common** (Tremont Street): The oldest public park in the US (established 1634). Used as a British military encampment during the Revolutionary War. Current use: public park, concerts, ice skating in winter.

**Massachusetts State House** (Beacon Street): Charles Bulfinch's 1798 Federal-style capitol building, with its gold-leaf dome. Tours available weekdays; the dome interior is one of the more impressive spaces in Boston.

**Park Street Church** (1 Park Street): An 1809 Federal-style church where "America" (My Country, 'Tis of Thee) was first publicly sung in 1831. William Lloyd Garrison gave his first anti-slavery speech here.

**Granary Burying Ground** (Tremont Street): The burial site of Paul Revere, Samuel Adams, John Hancock, and the victims of the Boston Massacre (1770). One of the oldest cemeteries in the US (1660).

**King's Chapel** (58 Tremont Street): The first Anglican (Church of England) church in Boston, built 1688. The adjacent burying ground is Boston's oldest (1630).

**Benjamin Franklin Statue / Old City Hall** (45 School Street): Franklin's birthplace plaque and the Victorian Old City Hall building.

**Old Corner Bookstore** (1 School Street): A 1712 building that housed a bookstore where Hawthorne, Emerson, Longfellow, and Thoreau published — the literary center of 19th-century American publishing.

**Old South Meeting House** (310 Washington Street): The site of the town meetings that led to the Boston Tea Party (December 16, 1773). Capacity: 1,000 people. The building has been preserved; an exhibit explains the pre-Revolution period.

**Old State House** (206 Washington Street): The seat of colonial government — the balcony from which the Declaration of Independence was first read to Bostonians in 1776. The Boston Massacre took place immediately outside (a circle of cobblestones marks the site). Museum inside: $12.

**Faneuil Hall** (Congress Street): The "Cradle of Liberty" — a market building and meeting hall where pre-Revolutionary public meetings were held. The building is still in use as a marketplace; the Great Hall on the second floor is restored to its 18th-century configuration. Free.

**Paul Revere House** (19 North Square): The oldest surviving building in downtown Boston (1680). Paul Revere's family home from 1770–1800, from which he departed for his midnight ride on April 18, 1775. Museum: $5.

**Old North Church** (193 Salem Street): The church from whose steeple two lanterns were hung on April 18, 1775 to signal that British troops were advancing by sea — the signal that triggered Paul Revere's ride. The phrase "One if by land, two if by sea" comes from Longfellow's poem about this event. The current church (1723) is the oldest standing church in Boston. Free.

**Copp's Hill Burying Ground** (Hull Street): The burial ground used by the colonial North End community. The British used gravestones for target practice during the siege of Boston.

**Charlestown Navy Yard and USS Constitution** (Charlestown, across the bridge): The USS Constitution ("Old Ironsides"), launched 1797 — the oldest commissioned warship in the US Navy, which earned its nickname from cannonballs bouncing off its oak hull during the War of 1812. Still in commission; tours free.

**Bunker Hill Monument** (Charlestown): A 221-step obelisk marking the site of the Battle of Bunker Hill (June 17, 1775) — one of the first major battles of the Revolution. The museum below has the historical context; climbing the obelisk provides views of Boston Harbor and the city.

## Harvard and MIT

Cambridge's universities are accessible to the public and historically significant in their own right.

**Harvard University** (Cambridge, Red Line to Harvard Station): Founded 1636 — the oldest university in the United States. The Harvard Yard (the original campus) is open to the public. The Harvard Art Museums (32 Quincy Street) hold collections of European, American, and Asian art; $20. The Widener Library (closed to non-Harvard visitors) has one of the largest university library collections in the world.

**MIT** (77 Massachusetts Avenue, Red Line to Kendall/MIT): The Massachusetts Institute of Technology (founded 1861) occupies a campus along the Charles River. The MIT Museum (265 Mass Ave) has robotics, holography, and science exhibitions. Several significant modernist buildings on campus are architectural landmarks.

## Boston Tea Party Ships and Museum

**306 Congress Street, Seaport** | $30

A re-creation of the two ships from which colonists dumped 342 chests of British East India Company tea into Boston Harbor on December 16, 1773. The museum uses first-person actor narration and participatory elements. More theatrical than a conventional museum but effective at conveying the sequence of events that made the Tea Party a historical turning point.
