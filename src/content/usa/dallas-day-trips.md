Dallas sits in the middle of north Texas — and the landscape for 50 miles in any direction is broadly flat. The dramatic landscapes (the Hill Country, Big Bend) require longer drives. But within a 2–3 hour radius, Dallas has some of the most specific Texas experiences available: Fort Worth's cattle-ranching history, Waco's unexpected museum scene, the rolling Cross Timbers landscape of the Arbuckle Mountains in Oklahoma, and the beginning of the Texas Hill Country.

---

## Fort Worth

**Distance**: 30 miles west via I-30  
**Driving time**: 30–45 minutes  
**TRE rail**: Dallas Union Station to Fort Worth T&P Station, 1 hour, $5  
**Best for**: Half-day or full day

Fort Worth is an entirely different city from Dallas — more Western, more grounded in its cattle-ranching and oil heritage, and in several specific areas more interesting than its larger eastern neighbor.

**Fort Worth Stockyards National Historic District**: The most specific Texas experience in the Dallas-Fort Worth area. The Stockyards were the commercial cattle-trading center of Texas from the 1870s through the mid-20th century. The historic district preserves the exchange building, the auction pens, and the main commercial strip.

The twice-daily cattle drive (11:30am and 4pm) is genuine: longhorn steers walk down Exchange Avenue, herded by working cowboys, every day of the year. It takes 15 minutes. It is one of the most specifically American spectacles available anywhere.

The **White Elephant Saloon** (106 E. Exchange Avenue) has been operating since 1884. The neon sign is visible from blocks away. The honky-tonk interior, the live Texas country music, and the Shiner Bock on draft are the entire point.

**Fort Worth Cultural District**: The surprise of Fort Worth — three world-class art museums within walking distance of each other:

- **Kimbell Art Museum** (free general admission; special exhibitions $18): One of the great small art museums in the world, in Louis Kahn's masterpiece building. The Velázquez, the El Greco, the Fra Angelico, and the Caravaggio are the highlights.
- **Modern Art Museum of Fort Worth** (free with suggested donation): Tadao Ando building on a reflecting pool. Excellent contemporary collection including significant Rothko, Richter, and Kiefer works.
- **Amon Carter Museum of American Art** (free): The definitive collection of Frederic Remington and Charles Russell Western American art.

**Food in Fort Worth**: **Joe T. Garcia's** (2201 N. Commerce Street) is a Tex-Mex institution operating since 1935 in a sprawling, hacienda-style building with outdoor patio dining. The margaritas are made from a single recipe that hasn't changed in decades. The combination plate feeds two people.

## Waco

**Distance**: 100 miles south via I-35  
**Driving time**: 1.5–2 hours  
**Best for**: Full day

Waco is a mid-size Texas city that became an unlikely day-trip destination after Chip and Joanna Gaines of HGTV's Fixer Upper turned it into a renovation and lifestyle tourism hub. The **Magnolia Market at the Silos** (601 Webster Avenue) is genuinely worth the drive if you're interested in design and home goods, but the real reason to visit Waco is different.

**Waco Mammoth National Monument** (6220 Steinbeck Bend Drive): A Columbian mammoth nursery herd fossil site — 24 mammoths who died together approximately 65,000 years ago, preserved in their original positions and now accessible via a covered excavation site. One of only two nursery herd mammoth sites in the world. Free entry, ranger-led tours $5. Unexpectedly excellent.

**Balcones Distilling** (225 S. 11th Street): One of the most acclaimed American craft whiskey distilleries, making Texas single malt and Texas pot still whiskey using locally grown grain. Tours and tastings available. The Texas single malt is genuinely distinctive and worth buying.

**Dr Pepper Museum** (300 S. 5th Street): Dr Pepper was invented in Waco in 1885 at the Old Corner Drug Store. The museum is in the original 1906 bottling plant. $8 admission. Quirky, specific, and more interesting than it sounds.

## Texas Hill Country (Fredericksburg)

**Distance**: 260 miles southwest via I-35 and US-290  
**Driving time**: 3.5–4 hours  
**Best for**: Overnight stay; possible long day trip if you leave very early

The Texas Hill Country begins roughly 200 miles southwest of Dallas and is the most dramatic landscape in the state's accessible tourist zone. Rolling limestone hills, live oak and juniper forests, spring-fed rivers, and the German immigrant heritage of the Fredericksburg corridor combine into something that doesn't feel like the flat north Texas plain at all.

**Fredericksburg**: A small city of 12,000 people that serves as the center of Hill Country wine country and antique shopping. The main street (Hauptstrasse) has shops, restaurants, and the **National Museum of the Pacific War** (340 E. Main Street) — the most significant WWII Pacific Theater museum in the country, located here because Admiral Chester Nimitz was born in Fredericksburg. $18 admission.

**Enchanted Rock State Natural Area**: A 1.825-billion-year-old granite dome, the largest such dome in the US, rising 425 feet above the surrounding terrain. The summit hike takes 45–60 minutes. Timed entry permits are required (book well ahead online; they sell out). $8/person.

**Wine country**: The US-290 corridor between Fredericksburg and Johnson City has 40+ wineries. The Texas Hill Country AVA produces interesting Tempranillo, Viognier, and Mourvèdre from heat-adapted vines. **William Chris Vineyards** and **Becker Vineyards** are the established references; $20–30 for tastings at most operations.

## Oklahoma (Turner Falls and Arbuckle Mountains)

**Distance**: 120 miles north via I-35  
**Driving time**: 1.5–2 hours  
**Best for**: Half-day

Turner Falls Park in the Arbuckle Mountains of southern Oklahoma is the closest swimming hole to Dallas that involves actual rocks and a waterfall. The 77-foot falls feed a swimming area in a natural rock basin. Park entry: $18/vehicle. The water is spring-fed and cold (18–20°C) — a genuine respite from Texas heat in July.

The Arbuckle Mountains are not dramatic by most standards — they are the oldest exposed mountain range in North America, worn down to gentle hills — but the landscape is noticeably different from the flat north Texas plain and the drive through the Arbuckle Wilderness area has its moments.

## McKinney and Sherman (North Texas Towns)

**Distance**: 30–50 miles north via US-75  
**Driving time**: 35–60 minutes  
**Best for**: Half-day

**McKinney**: A historic downtown with 1850s–1930s commercial architecture preserved along the main square. Antique stores, independent restaurants, and the feel of a Texas county seat town before the sprawl of the Metroplex. Free to walk; easy parking.

**Eisenhower Birthplace State Historic Site** (Denison, 75 miles north): The home where Dwight D. Eisenhower was born in 1890. $7 admission. For visitors interested in 20th-century American history, the site provides a coherent account of his life and career in the context of the house where it began.
