Dallas is hosting FIFA World Cup 2026 matches at AT&T Stadium in Arlington — and the experience will be unlike any other World Cup venue on the American roster. Texas does spectacle at a scale that embarrasses other states. AT&T Stadium, known locally as "Jerry World" after Cowboys owner Jerry Jones who commissioned it, is the largest domed stadium in the world. The video board suspended above the field is larger than the playing surface of most international stadiums. Dallas does not do things quietly.

The city around the stadium is a sprawling metroplex of 7.5 million people spread across the north Texas plains. There is no ocean. There is no dramatic natural landscape within easy reach. What Dallas has is food — specifically, the best barbecue in the United States and a Tex-Mex tradition built over a century — and a nightlife and cultural scene that most international visitors dramatically underestimate.

The matches take place in June and July. The weather will be hot. Texas hot: 35–42°C, low humidity compared to Miami but intense sun with very little shade. The AT&T Stadium's air conditioning is one of the most powerful HVAC systems ever installed in a public building. Inside, it is comfortable regardless of external conditions. Outside, plan accordingly.

## AT&T Stadium and Getting There

**AT&T Stadium** is located in Arlington, Texas — midway between Dallas and Fort Worth, 20 miles from downtown Dallas and 15 miles from downtown Fort Worth. Its standard capacity is 80,000, expandable to 105,000+ for major events. It opened in 2009 and cost $1.3 billion. The retractable roof, the 160-foot-wide end zone video boards, and the 600-ton center-hung scoreboard (the largest high-definition video board in the world when installed) are the defining features.

**The transit challenge**: Arlington has no public transit connections to the regional rail system. This is the fundamental match-day logistics reality for Dallas.

**Rideshare**: The primary option for most visitors. From downtown Dallas: 25–35 minutes normal, $35–55 normal pricing, $65–100 match-day surge. From Fort Worth: 15–20 minutes, $20–35.

**Driving**: The most common approach. I-30 West to AT&T Way exit. Stadium parking: $30–50 pre-purchased. Post-match on I-30 eastbound can add 30–60 minutes to exit.

**Trinity Railway Express (TRE)**: The commuter rail connecting Dallas Union Station and Fort Worth T&P Station does not stop at AT&T Stadium, but match-day shuttle buses will operate from the CentrePort/DFW Airport station on the TRE, approximately 3 miles from the stadium. From Dallas Union Station: 30–35 minutes to CentrePort, then shuttle. Total: approximately 50–60 minutes.

**From Dallas hotels**: The most practical World Cup configuration is staying in the Uptown/Downtown Dallas corridor, taking the DART light rail to Union Station, boarding TRE to CentrePort, and shuttling to the stadium. Buy a day pass on GoPass (DART's app) that covers both systems.

## Airports

**Dallas/Fort Worth International (DFW)**: One of the busiest airports in the world, 20 miles northwest of downtown Dallas. The **DART Orange Line** connects DFW Terminal A to downtown Dallas (Akard or Pearl/Arts District stations) in approximately 45–55 minutes, $2.50. Rideshare: $35–55 to downtown Dallas.

**Dallas Love Field (DAL)**: Southwest Airlines' hub, 6 miles from downtown. No direct rail connection. Rideshare: $15–25 to downtown Dallas, 15–20 minutes.

## Where to Stay

**Uptown Dallas**: The most walkable neighborhood for dining and nightlife, connected to downtown by the McKinney Avenue Trolley (free) and DART. Hotels $170–300/night. The area around McKinney Avenue and Cedar Springs has the highest restaurant density in the city.

**Downtown Dallas (Arts District area)**: Near DART rail stations, the Perot Museum, and the AT&T Performing Arts Center. Hotels $160–280/night. More practical than atmospheric for tourism.

**Deep Ellum**: The entertainment district east of downtown — the best nightlife neighborhood in Dallas. Limited hotels; primarily Airbnb and boutique options at $130–220/night.

**Addison**: A suburb north of Dallas with a dense restaurant strip on Belt Line Road. More practical for families; hotels $120–190/night. DART bus connections to the rail system.

## June and July in Dallas

Hot. Reliably, intensely hot. June averages 34–38°C; July averages 36–40°C. Dallas is inland and arid — none of Miami's humidity, but also no ocean breeze. The UV intensity is extreme and shade is sparse in most commercial areas.

There is essentially no rain June–July (May is wet; the dry season locks in for summer). You will not need an umbrella. You will need sunscreen, water, and access to air conditioning for midday hours (11am–4pm).

The saving grace: evening temperatures drop to 26–29°C and are genuinely pleasant. Outdoor dining, rooftop bars, and the Deep Ellum entertainment district are all excellent after 7pm. Dallas lives outdoors in the evenings specifically because the afternoons are brutal.

## Food Strategy

Dallas is a barbecue city first. The tradition here is Central Texas-influenced — smoked brisket, beef ribs, sausage — with the particular Dallas twist of a restaurant culture sophisticated enough to support excellent accompaniments.

**Pecan Lodge** (2702 Main Street, Deep Ellum): The most celebrated barbecue restaurant in Dallas. The brisket — 14-hour smoked over pecan and oak — is the reference point for the city. The "Trough" (a combination platter for four) is the correct order for a group. Arrive by 11am; the popular items sell out. Budget $20–30 per person.

**Cattleack Barbeque** (13628 Gamma Road, North Dallas): Considered by many food writers the best barbecue in Texas and therefore the best in the world. Open Thursday–Saturday only, limited hours, with lines forming hours before opening. The burnt ends and the turkey are the signatures alongside the brisket.

**Tex-Mex**: Dallas and Fort Worth have a Tex-Mex tradition separate from Los Angeles's Mexican food culture — flour tortillas, chile con queso, fajitas, and margaritas. **El Fenix** (the historic chain founded in Dallas in 1918) and **Meso Maya** represent the traditional and contemporary ends. Budget $15–25 per person.

**Uptown restaurant corridor**: The McKinney Avenue strip has every cuisine from Japanese to Peruvian at mid-range prices. **Knife** (the steak restaurant) and **Lucia** (Italian, considered one of the best restaurants in the city) are the fine dining anchors.

## Between Match Days

**Deep Ellum**: The entertainment neighborhood east of downtown was the home of Dallas blues music in the 1920s–30s (Blind Lemon Jefferson, Leadbelly recorded here). Today it has the best bars, live music venues, and street art in the city. Walk the main strip on Elm Street and Commerce Street.

**The Perot Museum of Nature and Science** (2201 N. Field Street): A Frank Gehry-adjacent building by Thom Mayne with excellent energy, geology, and Texas fossil exhibits. $25 admission.

**The Sixth Floor Museum at Dealey Plaza** (411 Elm Street): The museum in the Texas School Book Depository building from which Lee Harvey Oswald assassinated President Kennedy in 1963. Arguably the most significant museum in Dallas. $18 admission.

**Dallas Arboretum** (8525 Garland Road): 66 acres of gardens on White Rock Lake. Beautiful in June when the summer plantings are established. $15 admission, $15 parking.

**Fort Worth Stockyards**: 30 minutes west by car or TRE rail — the historic cattle-trading district with twice-daily cattle drives (genuinely), excellent Western wear stores, and the White Elephant Saloon. More authentic Western Texas atmosphere than anything in Dallas proper.

## Realistic Budget

Dallas is the most affordable major World Cup city. Budget travelers can manage $80–100/day (Airbnb or budget hotel, barbecue joints and Tex-Mex, DART rail). Mid-range runs $200–260/day. Match-day rideshare to Arlington is the main variable cost — budget $70–110 round trip per match. Hotels in Arlington are significantly cheaper ($120–180/night) than Dallas proper but offer less non-stadium experience.
