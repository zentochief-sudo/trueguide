The Pacific Ocean is one of LA's main arguments for existing, and in June and July — the months of the World Cup — it is at its most accessible. The water is cold by Mediterranean standards (18–20°C in summer), but the beaches themselves are spectacular: wide, clean, backed by mountains or palisades, and mostly free to access.

The 30-mile stretch from Malibu down through Santa Monica, Venice, Manhattan Beach, Hermosa Beach, and Redondo Beach is one of the best coastal corridors in the world for a day off between matches.

---

## The Weather Reality

June in LA is characterized by "June Gloom" — a marine layer that keeps mornings foggy and overcast. By noon most days it burns off. By mid-June it's reliably sunny by 11am. By mid-July, the marine layer has largely retreated and you get uninterrupted sunshine from morning to evening.

Water temperature: 17–20°C in June, 19–21°C in July. Cold by Southern European standards. Wetsuits are common for surfers; most swimmers and body-boarders don't bother in summer, but you won't stay in for long sessions.

UV intensity is extreme. The LA sun at sea level reflects off sand and water simultaneously. Bring SPF 50+, apply it twice, wear a hat if you're staying more than two hours.

## Santa Monica Beach

**Getting there**: Metro E Line to Downtown Santa Monica station, 5-minute walk west.

The most accessible major beach in LA and the default choice for World Cup visitors staying in the central neighborhoods. The beach itself is wide and well-maintained, backed by Palisades Park (a long, bluff-top linear park with city views). The Santa Monica Pier marks the northern end of a beach volleyball zone.

**What's here**: Bike rentals at the pier, volleyball courts, a trapeze school directly on the beach (a surreal sight), the Pacific Park amusement park on the pier, and the entire boardwalk system connecting north to the Palisades and south to Venice.

**The honest assessment**: Santa Monica Beach is beautiful but busy. On a summer Saturday, the parking lots fill by 10am and the volleyball courts are claimed early. For the cleanest experience, arrive before 9am or after 4pm.

## Venice Beach

**Getting there**: Metro E Line to 26th Street or Bergamot station, then ~20-minute walk south or take a bike.

Venice Beach is more interesting than Santa Monica Beach as a cultural experience. The **Venice Boardwalk** — a concrete walkway running parallel to the sand — has been the city's most concentrated display of human eccentricity since the 1970s. Rollerbladers, bodybuilders at **Muscle Beach** (the outdoor gym is a real facility used by real athletes), street artists, buskers, a snake charmer who has been there for years.

**Abbot Kinney Boulevard**, one block east of the beach, is the best shopping and restaurant street in the area. Go to the beach first, then walk to Abbot Kinney for lunch or coffee.

**The water**: The Venice break is a surf spot; you can watch surfers from the pier. The beach north of the pier is calmer for swimming.

**Bike path connection**: Venice is the midpoint of the **Marvin Braude Bike Trail** — renting a bike in Santa Monica and riding south to Venice Beach and beyond is one of the best free activities in the city. The full 22-mile trail continues to Torrance Beach.

## Manhattan Beach

**Getting there**: Metro C Line (Green Line) to Marine Ave station, then walk or take a local bus.

Manhattan Beach is what happens when a beach town gets wealthy: excellent restaurants, a compact walkable downtown, an iconic 928-foot pier, and some of the best beach volleyball in the country. The International Volleyball Hall of Fame is here; the Manhattan Beach Open (a major pro beach volleyball tournament) has been held here since 1960.

The water at Manhattan Beach is slightly warmer than Santa Monica and somewhat less crowded. The pier provides a straightforward reference point for meeting up.

**Where to eat**: The Strand (beachfront) has expensive restaurants with views. The downtown area one block inland has better food at lower prices. **Slay** for coffee, **Fishing with Dynamite** for seafood (book ahead), **Salt & Straw** for ice cream.

## El Matador State Beach (Malibu)

**Getting there**: Car (highly recommended), approximately 40 minutes from Santa Monica via PCH. No practical public transit option.

El Matador is the most dramatic beach in the LA area — stacked sea stacks, rock arches, and coves carved by surf into the Malibu bluffs. It requires a short scramble down a wooden staircase to reach, which keeps the casual crowds at bay. The water here is for photographs rather than serious swimming (the cove shape creates unpredictable currents), but the scenery justifies the drive.

**Parking**: $10/vehicle at the small state lot. Arrive before 9am on weekends or you will not find a space. A moderate hike along the bluff trail from a nearby turnout is a workable alternative if the lot is full.

**The PCH drive**: Pacific Coast Highway between Santa Monica and Malibu is itself worth the trip. Stop at any of the beach pull-outs heading north for different perspectives on the coastline.

**Combine with**: Drive further north on PCH to the Santa Ynez Mountains, or stop at **Surfrider Beach** in Malibu proper (consistent surf break, free parking is harder but beach is accessible). **Geoffrey's Malibu** restaurant has the best lunch view in the region.

## Hermosa Beach

**Getting there**: Metro C Line to Hermosa/Aviation station, 10-minute walk.

Hermosa is smaller and younger than Manhattan Beach — a pedestrianized Pier Plaza backed by bars and restaurants, beach volleyball courts, and a relaxed atmosphere. During June and July this area has a consistent party atmosphere that starts at the pier and extends outward.

For World Cup viewing, several bars around the Pier Plaza area will have outdoor screens. The atmosphere for watching matches here during a Brazil vs. Argentina game, for example, will be memorable.

**The Strand** continues through Hermosa — this is where the bike path is most pleasant, with the downtown backing right onto the beach.

---

## Zuma Beach (Malibu)

**Getting there**: Car, approximately 50 minutes from Santa Monica.

Zuma is LA County's largest beach park — 105 acres of flat, wide sand backed by parking lots and a lifeguard-managed swim zone. It lacks the dramatic scenery of El Matador but has the facilities that a full beach day requires: showers, restrooms, picnic areas, surfboard and wetsuit rentals.

The waves at Zuma are better for body-surfing and boogie boarding than El Matador. Lifeguards are on duty from 9am to sunset daily in summer.

---

## Redondo Beach

**Getting there**: Metro C Line to Redondo Beach station.

Redondo Beach is the southern anchor of the South Bay beach corridor. It has the most fishing-pier character of any LA beach town — the **Redondo Beach Pier** has fish market restaurants, bait shops, and a horseshoe harbor with moored boats.

The **International Boardwalk** around the pier has several good fish restaurants. **Quality Seafood** on the pier has been selling whole crabs and shrimp by the pound since 1953. The lunch at the outdoor fish market tables here — Dungeness crab, clam chowder, a cold beer — is one of the most LA experiences you can have that doesn't require a car trip to Malibu.

## Beach Practical Notes

**Parking**: All LA beaches have paid parking lots. Summer weekend rates: $5–15. Many lots fill before 10am. Either arrive early or plan to walk from transit.

**Free beach access**: All California beaches are legally required to be publicly accessible. You cannot be charged for walking on the sand itself — parking fees are for the lot only.

**Rip currents**: Present at all LA beaches. Swim between the flags when lifeguards are on duty. If caught in a rip current, swim parallel to shore rather than against it.

**Surf lessons**: Multiple schools operate at Santa Monica and Venice. A 90-minute group lesson costs $80–100 and includes board/wetsuit rental. You will not be surfing after one lesson, but you will be able to stand up in the white water.

**Sunset timing in June/July**: Sunset is approximately 7:45–8:05pm throughout the tournament period. The best sunset positions on the beach are facing west toward the Malibu headlands from Santa Monica, or at the pier itself. The evening light in LA in July is genuinely extraordinary.
