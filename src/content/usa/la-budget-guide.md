Los Angeles is not the cheapest World Cup city, and during the tournament it will be significantly more expensive than a normal June visit. Hotels near popular neighborhoods will charge 40–80% premiums for dates around match days. But unlike New York or San Francisco, LA has a functional low-cost tier for food and transport that can keep daily costs manageable for budget travelers.

The difference between a $90 day and a $300 day in LA often comes down to where you sleep and whether you take Metro or Uber.

---

## Accommodation

Accommodation is the single biggest variable in your LA budget. The World Cup premium is real — book as early as possible, ideally 3–6 months out.

**Hostels**: $45–75/night in a dorm. LA has a reasonable hostel market: **USA Hostels Hollywood**, **HI Los Angeles Santa Monica**, and **Samesun Hollywood** are the established options. Dorm sizes range from 6 to 12 beds. For comparison, the same quality hostel in Tokyo costs $25–35.

**Budget hotels and motels**: $120–180/night. The motel culture along roads like **Lincoln Boulevard** (Santa Monica to Culver City), **Hollywood Boulevard** (east of Highland), and in **Koreatown** offers functional, clean accommodation at reasonable rates. These are not glamorous, but they work.

**Mid-range hotels**: $200–280/night. Most 3-star hotels in Hollywood and Santa Monica fall here in normal times. During World Cup match weeks, expect the floor to rise to $250–320.

**Airbnb**: Can be excellent value if you're traveling in a group. A two-bedroom apartment in Koreatown or Silver Lake rents for $150–200/night — split between four people, that's $40–50 per person. Parking is often included, which saves $20–25/day versus hotels.

**The match-day premium**: Hotels within 5 miles of SoFi Stadium (Inglewood, Hawthorne, El Segundo) will charge the highest premiums around match dates but may be cheaper overall than Westside hotels. The tradeoff is a less convenient base for tourism.

## Getting Around

**Metro**: $1.75 per ride. A day pass costs $7. A weekly pass costs $25. If you base yourself in Hollywood or Koreatown and use Metro for most movements, you can run a full week of transport for $25–35 total (excluding match-day shuttles, which are covered by your match ticket).

**Rideshare**: The cost variable most likely to destroy a budget. Budget an average of $15–25 per ride under normal conditions. On match days, the stadium-adjacent surge means $60–120 each way. If you're taking rideshare to the stadium for every match, add $150–300 to your total travel cost for transport alone.

**Rental car**: A weekly rental from LAX costs $250–400. Add $15–25/day for parking. Total for 7 days: $360–575. This makes sense if you want to explore beyond Metro coverage (Malibu, the Valley, day trips) — it does not make sense if you're staying in one or two neighborhoods.

**Cycling**: The Marvin Braude Bike Trail along the coast is free. Bike rentals in Santa Monica and Venice run $20–40/day. E-scooters (Lime, Bird) cost $0.15–0.30/minute — a 20-minute ride is $3–6. Practical for last-mile connections from Metro stations.

## Food

This is where LA offers the best value of any major American city for travelers willing to eat where the city actually eats.

**$25–35/day eating well**: 
- Breakfast: Pastry and coffee from a bakery ($6–8) or eggs and tortillas from a taqueria ($5)
- Lunch: Grand Central Market ($10–14) or a taco truck ($8–10 for two tacos and a agua fresca)
- Dinner: Korean BBQ banchan + protein at a mid-tier K-Town restaurant ($18–22 per person), or a serious taqueria ($12–16)

This is the honest budget-eating reality of LA. You can eat genuinely well here for less than $35/day.

**$70–100/day eating at actual restaurants**:
- One mid-range restaurant per day ($40–55 for food and one drink)
- Casual lunches and cafe breakfasts for the other meals

**The splurge tier ($130–180/day food-only)**:
- One dinner at a serious restaurant (Bestia, Gjusta for brunch + dinner somewhere good, Park's BBQ for a full Korean BBQ session)
- Normal lunches and coffee otherwise

## Attractions

The majority of LA's best experiences are free or inexpensive.

**Free**:
- **Griffith Observatory** — free admission, $0 if you hike up from the neighborhood
- **Getty Center** — free admission (parking $25, Metro is free)
- **Griffith Park** — free, trails open all day
- **Venice Boardwalk** — free
- **All beaches** — free (parking $5–15)
- **Grand Central Market** — free to enter, pay for food
- **Farmers markets** — free to browse

**Low cost ($10–20)**:
- **LACMA (LA County Museum of Art)** — $25 general admission, $10 for students
- **Natural History Museum** — $18 general, free on certain days
- **Hollywood Bowl tours** — free self-guided tours on non-event days
- **Getty Villa** (Greek/Roman antiquities in Pacific Palisades) — free admission, requires advance timed-entry reservation

**Worth paying for**:
- **Universal Studios Hollywood** — $109+ (skip if time-limited; this takes a full day)
- **A guided hiking tour** — Canyon tours running $45–75 offer context the trails alone don't provide

## Tipping

The United States has a service industry tipping culture that visitors from Europe, Asia, and Latin America should understand before they arrive.

**Restaurants (sit-down)**: 18–20% is standard. 15% is acceptable for average service. 22–25% for exceptional service. The tip is calculated on the pre-tax total.

**Bars**: $1–2 per drink, or 20% on a tab.

**Rideshare**: Uber and Lyft suggest 15–20% in-app. You are not required to tip, but drivers rate you after the ride, and consistent non-tipping can affect your passenger score.

**Hotels**: $2–5/night for housekeeping (left daily, not at checkout). $2–5 for bellhops per bag.

**Taxis**: 15–20%.

**Coffee shops**: Optional but common (1–2% on the screen prompt is fine; decline if you prefer).

Add 15–20% of your total food and drink budget to account for tips. For a $70 food day, budget an additional $12–15 for tips.

## Sample Daily Budgets

**Budget traveler ($95–110/day)**:
- Hostel dorm: $55
- Food: $30 (taco trucks, Grand Central Market, farmers market)
- Transport: $7 (Metro day pass)
- Attraction: $0 (Griffith Park, beach)
- Tips: $6
- **Total: ~$98**

**Mid-range traveler ($220–260/day)**:
- Hotel (Hollywood or K-Town): $170
- Food: $70 (one mid-range restaurant, casual other meals)
- Transport: $20 (Metro + one rideshare)
- Attraction: $20 (LACMA or similar)
- Tips: $14
- **Total: ~$294** (slightly over, which is why booking accommodation early matters)

**Comfortable traveler ($320–380/day)**:
- Hotel (Santa Monica or WeHo): $280
- Food: $110 (one good dinner, quality lunches)
- Transport: $30 (Metro + occasional rideshare)
- Attraction: $25
- Tips: $22
- **Total: ~$467** — the "comfortable" tier in LA adds up fast. The key variable is accommodation.

## World Cup-Specific Costs

**Match ticket**: FIFA Group Stage tickets typically range $100–300 depending on category. Knockout stages run $200–600+. Buy through FIFA's official ticketing platform.

**Match-day transport add-on**: Rideshare to stadium on match days adds $60–120 each way (or $0 if you take Metro with your TAP card).

**Pre/post-match food and drink near stadium**: Budget $25–40 for a meal at Hollywood Park complex restaurants.

## Where LA Actually Saves You Money

Compared to New York and San Francisco, LA is cheaper in two meaningful ways:

1. **Food**: The taco truck and Korean BBQ ecosystem means you can eat extremely well at the $10–20 per meal price point. In NYC, that price point is a slice of pizza and a cup of soup.

2. **Free outdoor activities**: The beaches, parks, hiking trails, and free museums provide a full activity calendar without spending anything. A day at El Matador State Beach in Malibu costs the price of gas and parking ($8).

The expensive parts of LA — hotels, rideshare on match days, nightlife in WeHo — are avoidable with planning. The good parts — tacos, beaches, Korean BBQ, the Getty — are either cheap or free.
