Los Angeles is hosting FIFA World Cup 2026 matches at SoFi Stadium in Inglewood — and the city around it is unlike any other World Cup venue on this list. This is a horizontal city of 13 million people spread across 500 square miles of basin, canyon, and coastline, held together by freeways and the stubborn belief that the next neighborhood might be better than this one. Understanding how it works before you arrive will save you from spending half your trip sitting in traffic.

The matches take place in June and July. The weather is the best of any World Cup host city: dry, sunny, 26–32°C, almost no rain. The Pacific Ocean is 20 minutes from downtown. The food is exceptional. The traffic is as advertised.

## SoFi Stadium and Getting There

**SoFi Stadium** opened in 2020 in Inglewood, a city that is technically separate from Los Angeles but surrounded by it on all sides. Its capacity is 70,240, expandable to approximately 100,000 for the World Cup. It has a translucent roof and a 70,000-square-foot double-sided video board. It is one of the most expensive stadiums ever built ($5.5 billion) and it shows.

The stadium does not have ideal public transport for a World Cup context. The **Metro K Line** (Crenshaw/LAX Line) has a stop at Inglewood — Fairview Heights, roughly 1.5 miles from the stadium. On match days, shuttles and enhanced bus service will operate from that station. The full journey from downtown LA by Metro is approximately 45–55 minutes.

The other option is **rideshare**, but on match days Uber and Lyft prices surge severely — budget $60–120 each way from Hollywood or Santa Monica. If you're driving, parking lots near the stadium start at $50–80 on regular game days and will be higher for World Cup matches; the Google Maps routing algorithm during peak egress can add 90 minutes to a simple 10-mile trip.

The most practical strategy: take Metro to the K Line, pick up the shuttle. Buy a **TAP card** at any Metro station, load $20–30 on it, and use it throughout your trip.

## Where to Stay

The city's geography means your neighborhood choice determines your experience. There is no single "best" area — it depends entirely on what you're prioritizing.

**Santa Monica** is the most comfortable base for first-timers. The beach is immediately accessible, the **Metro E Line (Expo Line)** connects it to downtown in about 45 minutes, and the 3rd Street Promenade has every practical service you need. Hotels run $200–350/night in summer.

**Hollywood** puts you on the B Line (Red Line subway) with direct access to downtown and Union Station. It's more central than Santa Monica, with more nightlife options and a wider range of hotel prices ($150–280/night). The Walk of Fame is overrated; the transit access is not.

**Koreatown** (K-Town) is underused by international visitors and excellent. It's the densest neighborhood in Los Angeles, has 24-hour restaurants, is on the B and D Metro lines, and hotels run $120–200/night. The Korean BBQ and ramen options within walking distance of any hotel are exceptional.

**Brickell equivalent in LA: Brickell**: For adults who want walkable urban density, Downtown LA (DTLA) and neighboring **Brickell** are developing fast. The Arts District has the best restaurant-to-square-meter ratio in the city. Hotels from $170–280/night.

**West Hollywood (WeHo)** is central, walkable by LA standards, has excellent restaurants along Santa Monica Boulevard and Melrose, and sits between Hollywood and Beverly Hills. No Metro station, but rideshare is practical at these distances.

## June and July in LA

The weather is genuinely perfect. June and early July are characterized by "June Gloom" — a marine layer that keeps mornings overcast and temperatures mild (20–24°C) before burning off by noon. By mid-July the marine layer recedes and temperatures reach 30–34°C inland. The coast stays cooler throughout.

There is essentially no rain between May and October. You will not need an umbrella. You will need sunscreen, water, and shade.

The city is at its most alive in summer. Outdoor concerts at the Hollywood Bowl, beach culture in full swing, rooftop bars open until 2am. The World Cup will add an international energy that LA, with its enormous Latin American population, will absorb without much friction.

## Getting Around

Accept the following truth early: **Los Angeles is easier to navigate than its reputation suggests, but you need to pick your strategy and commit to it**.

**If you stay on the Westside (Santa Monica, Venice, Hollywood)**: the E Line and B Line cover most of your needs. You can exist here without a car.

**If you want to explore broadly** (East LA, Pasadena, the San Fernando Valley): you need a rental car or a very high tolerance for rideshare costs. Renting a car at LAX is straightforward; weekly rates run $300–500. Avoid driving during the 7–10am and 4–8pm rush windows — these are not guidelines, they are minimums for an extra 30–50% journey time.

**Parking reality**: expect to pay $15–30/day anywhere near Hollywood, Santa Monica, or Beverly Hills. Many Airbnbs and budget hotels do not include parking.

## Food Strategy

Los Angeles has the best Mexican food outside Mexico, the best Korean food outside Korea, excellent Japanese food (particularly in Torrance and the San Gabriel Valley), and a farm-to-table Californian restaurant culture that is genuinely world-class.

The mistake most visitors make is eating in tourist zones. A $4 taco from a truck in Boyle Heights is better than a $22 taco at a hotel restaurant in Beverly Hills. **Grand Central Market** in downtown LA is the best single location for variety and value — a 1917 market hall with vendors serving everything from carnitas to Thai to excellent coffee.

**In-N-Out Burger** is not overrated. Get a Double-Double Animal Style. It costs $6.

## Between Match Days

The city rewards slower exploration. Between matches, the itinerary writes itself: a morning at a farmers market (Santa Monica Wednesday, Hollywood Sunday), an afternoon at the Getty (free entry, $25 parking), a sunset at Griffith Observatory (free), dinner somewhere on Sunset Boulevard or in Silver Lake.

If you have a full free day: drive north on Pacific Coast Highway to Malibu, stop at **El Matador State Beach** for dramatic rock formations, continue to a winery in the Santa Ynez Valley if time allows, return via the 101.

The **Getty Villa** in Pacific Palisades (separate from the Getty Center) houses the finest collection of Greek and Roman antiquities in the western United States. Free, requires advance timed-entry reservation.

## Realistic Budget

Los Angeles sits between New York and Miami in cost. Budget travelers can manage on $100–130/day (hostel, tacos, Metro, free beaches). A mid-range trip with a decent hotel, restaurants, and occasional rideshare runs $220–300/day. The World Cup premium on accommodation near match dates will add 40–80% to hotel rates — book early and consider staying further from the stadium.

The one area where spending more is consistently justified: food. A $150 dinner at a good restaurant in LA is one of the best value propositions of any major city in America.
