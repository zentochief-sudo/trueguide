Miami sits at the geographic crossroads of several completely different landscapes. Twenty miles to the west, the city gives way to the Florida Everglades — the largest subtropical wilderness in the United States. Sixty miles to the south, the Florida Keys stretch into the clear waters of the Gulf of Mexico. Thirty miles to the north, Fort Lauderdale and Palm Beach have their own distinct beach and cultural identities.

Between World Cup matches, these options provide the kind of extreme landscape change that helps a week in one city feel like a broader journey.

## The Florida Everglades

**Distance from Miami**: The main entrance (Ernest F. Coe Visitor Center) is 35 miles southwest of Downtown Miami.  
**Driving time**: 50–70 minutes via the Florida Turnpike south to SR-9336 west.  
**Public transit**: No practical option — car or tour is required.  
**Best for**: Half-day or full day

The Everglades is a 1.5-million-acre subtropical wilderness — the only place in the world where alligators and crocodiles coexist, home to 350+ bird species, and one of the few places in the United States where the natural environment is genuinely otherworldly in its strangeness.

**What to see**:

**Anhinga Trail** (Ernest F. Coe area): A 0.8-mile boardwalk trail through sawgrass marsh that provides the best wildlife viewing in the park for casual visitors. Named for the anhinga bird (which dries its wings in the sun after diving, giving it a pterodactyl-like silhouette). American alligators rest under the boardwalk without concern for human presence. In June–July, the juvenile alligators and wading birds are at their most active. Entirely flat, takes 30–45 minutes.

**Mrazek Pond** (9 miles west of the Coe Visitor Center): During dry season (winter), this concentration pond is famous for extraordinary bird photography. In summer (wet season, June–October), the birds disperse — but the scenery is still excellent and the insects are manageable with repellent.

**Eco Pond** (near Flamingo, the park's southern terminus): A brackish pond where roseate spoonbills, wood storks, and tricolored herons are regularly present year-round.

**Airboat tours**: The classic Everglades experience — a flat-bottomed boat driven by an aircraft-style propeller, skimming over the sawgrass at speed. Multiple operators on the northern (Tamiami Trail) edge of the park and in Everglades City offer 1–2 hour tours at $35–55. These tours reach the wildlife; the park's official trails offer a quieter experience.

**Summer caution**: The Everglades in June–July is hot (33–36°C), extremely humid, and full of mosquitoes. Bring long-sleeved clothing, DEET-based repellent, and a hat. The afternoon thunderstorms that hit Miami also hit the Everglades, typically arriving around 2–4pm. Morning visits (starting by 7am) are significantly more comfortable and the wildlife is more active.

**Park entrance**: $35/vehicle, valid 7 days.

## The Florida Keys

**Distance from Miami**: Key Largo begins 60 miles south of Downtown Miami. Key West is 160 miles from Miami.  
**Driving time**: Key Largo: 60–75 minutes. Marathon: 2.5 hours. Key West: 3.5–4 hours via US-1 (the Overseas Highway).  
**Public transit**: No practical option — car required.  
**Best for**: Full day (Key Largo to Marathon), overnight for Key West

The Florida Keys are a 120-mile chain of coral islands connected by the Overseas Highway — the only road in America that runs over open ocean. The highway's famous bridges, including the Seven Mile Bridge between Marathon and Big Pine Key, are engineering spectacles and produce some of the most cinematic driving in the country.

**Key Largo**: The northernmost island has the best accessible snorkeling and diving. **John Pennekamp Coral Reef State Park** (MM 102.5, Overseas Highway) has a glass-bottom boat tour ($30) and snorkel rentals for the nearshore reefs ($30–45 including equipment and a boat tour). The water visibility here is 5–15 meters; the coral formations are the most accessible example of living reef in the continental US.

**Islamorada**: The fishing capital of the Florida Keys. Multiple charter boat operations offer half-day fishing trips ($150–200 per person) for mahi-mahi, wahoo, and tuna. **Robbie's Marina** has an unusual walk-on experience: a dock where you buy a bucket of fish and feed tarpon (100+ pound silver kings) by hand from the pier. $5 to feed; $4 entry to the dock.

**Marathon and the Seven Mile Bridge**: The midpoint of the Keys. The Old Seven Mile Bridge (a 1912 railway bridge converted to a pedestrian walkway) runs parallel to the current highway and provides a 2.2-mile walk above the open water of the Gulf of Mexico. The view from the midpoint — water in all directions, nothing else — is disorienting and extraordinary.

**Key West**: Too far for a day trip from Miami (7–8 hours round trip by car). Worth the overnight stay for the particular atmosphere: a subtropical island city with an active LGBTQ+ community, a strong literary history (Hemingway House is a genuine museum at 907 Whitehead Street), and the Duval Street bar culture that has been drawing people south since the 1970s. The sunset celebration at Mallory Square (acrobats, musicians, vendors gathering at the waterfront nightly as the sun goes down) is a free nightly event that summarizes Key West's approach to time.

## Fort Lauderdale

**Distance from Miami**: 30 miles north via I-95 or US-1.  
**Driving time**: 30–45 minutes under normal conditions.  
**Tri-Rail**: Miami Central → Fort Lauderdale: 45 minutes, $5.35. Fort Lauderdale Airport → Miami: 35 minutes.  
**Best for**: Half-day or relaxed full day

Fort Lauderdale is Miami's slightly calmer, less expensive northern neighbor. It has its own beach (the Broadwalk — 2.5 miles of beachfront promenade), its own downtown, and a specific character as Florida's "Venice of America" — the city has 165+ miles of navigable waterways that wind through residential and commercial areas.

**Las Olas Boulevard**: The main commercial street in downtown Fort Lauderdale. Outdoor restaurants, independent shops, galleries, and the cultural institutions. The **NSU Art Museum Fort Lauderdale** (1 E. Las Olas) has a strong collection of Pop Art (William Glackens collection) and Latin American contemporary art. $15 admission.

**Fort Lauderdale Beach**: Less crowded than South Beach, with the same Atlantic Atlantic conditions. The Broadwalk is flat and excellent for cycling. **Hugh Taylor Birch State Park** (3109 E. Sunrise Boulevard) provides a rare patch of coastal hammock forest immediately behind the beach.

**The Las Olas Waterway**: Water taxis run up and down the New River and the Intracoastal Waterway, connecting the beach to downtown to the marina district. A 90-minute narrated water taxi tour gives context to Fort Lauderdale's unusual geography. $28.

**For Hard Rock Stadium visitors**: Fort Lauderdale is closer to Hard Rock Stadium than South Beach. Hotels here are $120–200/night versus $250–380 in South Beach. A Fort Lauderdale base with Tri-Rail access to both Miami and the stadium vicinity is the best budget-travel configuration for the World Cup.

## Palm Beach

**Distance from Miami**: 70 miles north via I-95.  
**Driving time**: 70–90 minutes.  
**Tri-Rail**: Miami Central → West Palm Beach station: 1.5–1.75 hours, $7.40.  
**Best for**: Full day

Palm Beach is America's most concentrated display of wealth — a small barrier island with the highest per-capita income in the country, the Breakers hotel (opened 1896, $1,200+/night), and Worth Avenue (a short street with Chanel, Hermès, and Tiffany in architecturally significant buildings).

**Worth Avenue**: Even for visitors with no intention of shopping, a 20-minute walk along Worth Avenue and its "vias" (small pedestrian alleys off the main street, designed in the 1920s) is architecturally interesting. The Addison Mizner-designed buildings set the template for Mediterranean Revival architecture in Florida.

**The Norton Museum of Art** (1450 S. Dixie Highway, West Palm Beach — immediately across the bridge from Palm Beach): A genuine art museum with a strong collection across American, European, and Chinese art. $18 admission.

**Mounts Botanical Garden** (531 N. Military Trail, West Palm Beach): 14 acres of tropical and subtropical plants. Free admission. The best free botanical experience in South Florida.

**Singer Island** (15 miles north of Palm Beach): A barrier island with the most uncrowded Atlantic beach in the greater Miami area. **John D. MacArthur Beach State Park** has a 2-mile beach, a nature center, and excellent snorkeling at the southern end. $6/vehicle.
