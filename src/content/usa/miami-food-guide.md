Miami's food culture is built on the successive waves of Latin American immigration that have made it the most Latin city in the United States. Cuban food arrived first, in the 1950s and 60s. Colombian, Venezuelan, Argentine, Peruvian, Brazilian, and Nicaraguan cuisines followed in waves through the 70s, 80s, and 90s. The result is a food city that, for Latin American cuisine specifically, is unmatched anywhere in North America.

The practical consequence for World Cup visitors: you can eat Peruvian ceviche on Monday, a proper Cuban sandwich on Tuesday, a wood-fired Argentine asado on Wednesday, and Venezuelan arepas on Thursday — all within a few miles of each other, all at prices well below what the same food costs in New York.

## Cuban Food

Cuban food is the foundation of Miami's food culture and the category that justifies the deepest exploration.

**The Cuban sandwich**: A pressed sandwich of roast pork, ham, Swiss cheese, yellow mustard, and pickles on Cuban bread. The bread — baked with a palmetto leaf pressed into the top to create the characteristic crease — is specific to South Florida and Tampa. The sandwich is pressed in a plancha (flat press) until the bread crisps and the cheese melts.

**The correct version**: The filling should have both roast pork and ham. The mustard is yellow, not Dijon. The pickles are sliced thin. The bread is the whole point — if it's a generic sandwich roll, it's not the real thing.

**Versailles Restaurant** (3555 SW 8th Street, Little Havana): The most famous Cuban restaurant in the city. The Cuban sandwich here is $12–14. The atmosphere — a large, mirror-lined room that has been feeding Miami's Cuban community since 1971 — is worth the trip. Order the Cuban sandwich, the black bean soup, and the media noche (a sweeter, egg-dough version of the Cuban sandwich).

**El Rey de las Fritas** (1821 SW 8th Street): The specialists in the Cuban frita — a burger made with a beef and chorizo patty, topped with shoestring fried potatoes and a specific onion-tomato sauce, on a small soft roll. $6–8. One of the most underrated things you can eat in Miami.

**Sanguich de Miami** (2057 SW 8th Street): A newer operation focused exclusively on high-quality Cuban sandwiches. The bread is made in-house. The roast pork is slow-cooked. Worth the line.

**The coffee ritual**: The Cuban coffee culture in Miami is specific and worth understanding. A café cubano is a small shot of espresso sweetened with sugar foam (the sugar is whipped with the first drops of espresso). A cortadito is the same with a small amount of steamed milk. A café con leche is the same with a full glass of hot milk. A colada is a larger serving of café cubano in a small styrofoam cup with a stack of small plastic cups for sharing — the Miami communal coffee ritual. All of this costs $1.50–3 from any Little Havana ventanita (walk-up window).

## Peruvian Food

Miami has the best Peruvian food outside of [Peru](/peru) and Lima's diaspora cities. The Peruvian community here has been established long enough to support serious restaurants, not just casual operations.

**Ceviche**: The Peruvian version — raw fish cured in lime juice (leche de tigre) with red onion, cilantro, and ají amarillo — is the flagship dish. The leche de tigre (the citrus cure liquid drained from the fish) is served alongside the ceviche as a shot or soup. It has a reputation as a hangover cure that is largely accurate.

**La Mar by Gastón Acurio** (Mandarin Oriental Hotel, 500 Brickell Key Drive): The Miami outpost of Lima's most famous chef. The ceviche is excellent; the menu is extensive. This is a splurge ($60–80 per person) but represents Peruvian cuisine at its best. The bay views from the terrace are exceptional.

**Cvi.Che 105** (105 NE 3rd Avenue, Downtown): The mid-range reference point for Peruvian food in Miami. Multiple ceviche variations, causas (chilled potato terrine), lomo saltado, and the classic leche de tigre shots. Budget $30–45 per person.

**MC Kitchen** and multiple smaller Peruvian operations in Doral (a Miami suburb with a very large Peruvian community, 20 minutes west of Downtown) offer more local, less visitor-facing versions of the cuisine.

## Colombian Food

The Colombian community in Miami is large enough to sustain an entire neighborhood food culture in areas like Doral and Kendall.

**Ajiaco**: A Colombian chicken and potato soup with corn on the cob, serving a thick broth thickened by the three varieties of potato traditionally used. A full bowl with avocado and cream is a meal.

**Bandeja paisa**: The defining plate of Colombian food — a large oval platter with red beans, white rice, ground meat, chicharrón (fried pork belly), fried egg, plantain, avocado, and arepa. It is not a meal; it is a commitment.

**La Camaronera** (1952 SW 8th Street, Little Havana): Technically focused on seafood rather than Colombian-specific, but the shrimp in garlic sauce and the fried fish options here represent the broader Latin American seafood tradition extremely well. Inexpensive, local, always crowded.

**El Palacio de los Jugos** (5721 W. Flagler Street): A Latin American market and cafeteria hybrid that is one of the most specific Miami food experiences available. Fresh-squeezed tropical fruit juices, fried yuca, tamales, whole roast pork, and dozens of hot dishes behind a counter. Budget $10–15 for a full meal. This is where Miami's Latin American community actually eats on weekday lunches.

## Venezuelan Food

Venezuela's political crisis brought a significant wave of Venezuelan immigration to Miami beginning in the 2010s, and with it, a Venezuelan food culture that was previously minimal in the city.

**Arepas**: The Venezuelan national food — cornmeal patties split and filled with everything from reina pepiada (chicken and avocado) to pernil (slow-roasted pork) to pabellón (shredded beef with black beans). Unlike Colombian arepas (thinner, usually eaten plain alongside other food), Venezuelan arepas are thick and specifically designed as the bread/vessel for a filling.

**La Latina** (multiple locations in Brickell and Coral Gables): The most established Venezuelan restaurant chain in Miami. The arepas here are properly thick, the fillings are authentic. A full arepa with a juice costs $12–16.

**Pepperoni Venezuelan** (multiple locations): Quick-service Venezuelan food at honest prices. The cachapas (corn pancakes with hand-pulled string cheese) are the item to try.

## Argentine Steakhouses

Miami has a significant Argentine community, particularly in Brickell and Coral Gables, and with it, a serious tradition of Argentine-style steakhouses (parrillas).

**La Leche** (1080 Alton Road, South Beach): A well-regarded Argentine steakhouse with wood-fired grill. The tira de asado (short ribs cut thinly across the bone, Argentine style) is the signature. Budget $60–80 per person.

**Cibo Wine Bar** (45 SW 9th Street, Brickell): Italian rather than Argentine, but the steak program here is among the best in Miami for the price-to-quality ratio. Dry-aged beef, proper cooking temperatures. Budget $45–70 per person.

**El Novillo** (6830 Bird Road, South Miami): The oldest Argentine steakhouse in Miami — a family-run operation that hasn't been renovated since 1990 and doesn't need to be. The picanha (rump cap, the Brazilian-Argentine crossover cut) is the reason to go. Budget $35–55 per person.

## Seafood

Miami's position at the junction of the Atlantic and the Gulf of [Mexico](/mexico) means genuinely excellent local seafood.

**Stone crab**: Available October–May (the claws are harvested from live crabs and the crabs returned to the ocean — the season ends before the World Cup, so this is not a June–July option). Worth knowing about for early June matches near the season's end.

**Florida spiny lobster**: The season opens in August, so it also misses the main tournament. However, frozen or farmed versions appear on menus throughout summer.

**Mahi-mahi (dorado), grouper, snapper, and swordfish**: All local, all in season, all excellent. The best fish restaurants cook the catch of the day — ask what came in that morning.

**Monty's Raw Bar** (2550 S. Bayshore Drive, Coconut Grove): A waterfront institution with excellent oysters, raw bar selections, and grilled fish. The outdoor deck on Biscayne Bay is the place to sit. Budget $35–55 per person.

**Garcia's Seafood Grille** (398 NW North River Drive, Overtown): A dockside fish market and restaurant that has been here since 1966. You can watch the fishing boats unload from the terrace. The fried snapper and the grouper sandwich are the standards. Budget $20–35 per person.

## Where Not to Eat

**Ocean Drive, South Beach**: Every restaurant on the oceanfront strip of Ocean Drive is expensive and mediocre, designed for tourists who won't return. The food is not worth the view price.

**Bayside Marketplace** (Downtown waterfront): A tourist trap in mall format.

**Most hotel restaurants**: Exceptions exist, but the default hotel restaurant in Miami charges for the address.

## Practical Eating Notes

**Meal times**: Miami eats late — later than most US cities. Dinner restaurants don't fill until 9–10pm. If you arrive at 7pm, you'll often have the restaurant to yourself (which is a feature, not a bug, on match days).

**Lunch deals**: Many Brickell and Downtown restaurants offer lunch specials at $15–20 that represent their full menu at half the dinner price. This is the best value in the city's serious restaurant tier.

**Tipping**: Standard US 18–20% at sit-down restaurants. The additional Florida sales tax (7%) is applied to restaurant bills — tip on the pre-tax amount.
