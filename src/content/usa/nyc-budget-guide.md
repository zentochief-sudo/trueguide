New York is the most expensive city on the World Cup 2026 roster. A comfortable mid-range trip here costs significantly more than in Miami or Los Angeles, and the tournament premium — 50–80% hotel rate increases on match weeks — pushes costs higher still. At the same time, New York has a functional cheap tier for food and transit that makes it possible to have a genuinely good time on a constrained budget.

The key insight: accommodation drives everything. The distance between a $90/day trip and a $400/day trip in New York is largely about where you sleep. Everything else — food, transit, attractions — has cheaper and more expensive versions within arm's reach of each other.

---

## Accommodation

New York City accommodation prices are among the highest in the world, and World Cup demand will push them further.

**Hostels**: $50–90/night for a dorm bed. Manhattan hostels include **HI New York City** (Amsterdam Avenue, Upper West Side), **The Local NYC** (Long Island City, Queens), and **Harlem YMCA** (for budget-focused travelers comfortable with the YMCA format). Queens hostels tend to be $10–15 cheaper per night and are often 20–30 minutes from Midtown by subway.

**Budget hotels**: $140–220/night. The lowest floor of Manhattan hotel pricing. Properties in **Long Island City** (Queens), **Astoria** (Queens), and **Williamsburg** (Brooklyn) offer more room and better quality at these price points than equivalent Manhattan properties.

**Mid-range hotels**: $250–380/night in normal Manhattan summer periods. During match weeks for knockout rounds, expect the floor to rise to $300–450. The **Pod Hotels** (39th Street and 42nd Street) are the best-designed budget-Manhattan options at $180–250.

**Airbnb**: More limited in NYC than in other cities due to strict short-term rental laws — legally, the host must be present for rentals under 30 days. Enforcement varies, but legal full-apartment rentals are rare. Rooms within apartments (host-present) are legal and available, typically $100–180/night in good neighborhoods.

**The World Cup premium**: Book accommodation 3–6 months in advance. The semifinal and Final dates at MetLife will have hotel rates at 2–3× normal levels across Manhattan and much of Brooklyn. For those matches specifically, consider Long Island City or Astoria hotels, which are high value and one subway stop from the city.

## Transit

Transit is where New York offers its best value relative to other major cities.

**Subway 7-day unlimited pass**: $34. This covers all subway rides and most buses for the week. Add $20–30 for two NJ Transit round trips to MetLife and you have a week's full transit coverage for $54–64 total.

**OMNY tap**: $2.90/ride, with automatic weekly cap of $34 (equivalent to an unlimited pass) for 11+ rides in a week. Use any contactless card — no need for a MetroCard.

**Single-day transit estimate**: $7–10 for the subway unlimited day pass (check if current), or approximately $9–15 in individual OMNY taps for typical tourist movement patterns (5–8 rides).

**Rideshare**: Budget $15–30 per ride for standard in-Manhattan trips. Match-day New Jersey surge: $65–110 each way. The subway is always faster and cheaper within Manhattan — rideshare makes sense primarily for airport trips or late-night situations when the subway is too slow.

**Citi Bike**: $4.50 per 30-minute ride or a $19 day pass. Worth using for short hops in good weather.

## Food

New York food divides cleanly between expensive (restaurants, hotel food, tourist zone dining) and affordable (the bodega sandwich, the pizza slice, the Chinatown dumpling, the halal cart). Both tiers exist in every neighborhood.

**$30–40/day eating well**:
- Breakfast: Bodega egg-and-cheese on a roll ($4–6) or bagel with cream cheese ($6–8)
- Lunch: Pizza by the slice ($4–6 each, 2 slices) or Chinatown dumplings ($3–10)
- Dinner: Dominican rice and chicken in Washington Heights ($12–16) or dim sum in Flushing ($15–20)

**$80–100/day eating at real restaurants**:
- One mid-range restaurant per day ($45–55 with one drink)
- Cheap breakfasts and occasional street food for other meals

**The splurge tier ($150–200/day food-only)**:
- One serious dinner (Via Carota, Lilia, any serious Brooklyn restaurant) + normal other meals
- Katz's Delicatessen adds $28–35 for a once-in-a-trip pastrami sandwich

## Attractions

The expensive attractions in New York are genuinely expensive. The free ones are excellent.

**Free**:
- **Central Park** — 843 acres, no entry fee
- **The High Line** — free, open 7am–10pm
- **Brooklyn Bridge walk** — free
- **Brooklyn Bridge Park** — free
- **Staten Island Ferry** — free, excellent views of Manhattan and the Statue of Liberty
- **Most NYC public spaces** — SummerStage concerts in Central Park are free; many museum evening events are free

**Pay-what-you-wish**:
- **The Metropolitan Museum of Art** — $30 suggested, but you can pay less; technically suggested for NYC residents, required for tourists at $30

**Worth paying for**:
- **Top of the Rock** (Rockefeller Center) — $40+, but arguably better Manhattan views than the Empire State Building observation deck (you can see the Empire State Building from here)
- **Empire State Building** — $44+ for the main deck. The classic, worth it for the view quality and history
- **One World Observatory** (World Trade Center tower) — $44+, best views of Lower Manhattan and New York Harbor

**Skip or think twice**:
- **Statue of Liberty ferry** — $24–30 round trip, 2–3 hour commitment; the Staten Island Ferry offers similar views for free
- **Circle Line boat tours** — $50+; the East River ferry does portions of this route for $4

## Tipping

US tipping culture is more pronounced in New York than anywhere else in the country.

**Sit-down restaurants**: 20% is the standard. 18% for adequate service. 22–25% for good service. Tip on the pre-tax total.

**Bars**: $1–2 per drink. Open tabs: 20% at close.

**Taxis**: 20%.

**Rideshare**: 15–20% suggested in-app.

**Hotels**: $3–5/night for housekeeping, $3–5/bag for bellhops.

**Coffee shops**: Optional — tipping screens appear everywhere. $1 on a counter service coffee is reasonable.

Budget approximately 20% on top of all food and drink spending for tips.

## Sample Daily Budgets

**Budget traveler ($115–135/day)**:
- Hostel dorm (Queens): $65
- Food: $35 (bodega breakfast, pizza/Chinatown lunch, halal cart dinner)
- Transit: $10 (subway unlimited day pass)
- Attraction: $0 (Central Park, High Line, Brooklyn Bridge)
- Tips: $7
- NJ Transit on match days: add $18–20
- **Total: ~$117** non-match days, ~$135 on match days

**Mid-range traveler ($280–340/day)**:
- Hotel (Williamsburg or Midtown budget): $220
- Food: $80 (one restaurant, other meals casual)
- Transit: $15
- Attraction: $30 (one paid museum or observation deck)
- Tips: $16
- **Total: ~$361** — already pressing the upper end of "mid-range" in NYC

**Comfortable traveler ($420–500/day)**:
- Hotel (Midtown, decent property): $350
- Food: $120 (one good dinner, quality lunches and breakfasts)
- Transport: $25 (subway + occasional taxi)
- Attraction: $40
- Tips: $24
- **Total: ~$559** — the comfortable tier in NYC is expensive by global standards

## World Cup-Specific Costs

**Match ticket**: Group stage $100–300; knockout stages $200–600+; the Final $500–1,500+ (official channels). Buy exclusively through FIFA's official ticketing platform.

**NJ Transit match day**: $14–20 round trip per match.

**Pre/post-match food near Penn Station**: Budget $20–35 for a sit-down meal in Midtown before the match.

## Where NYC Saves You Money Versus Expectations

New York's reputation for extreme expense is accurate at the hotel and restaurant level but understated at the street-food and transit level. The subway gives you access to the entire city for $2.90. The bodega sandwich is a legitimate $5 breakfast. The pizza slice at noon is the most honest value proposition in American food. 

The visitors who spend the most in New York are the ones who never leave their hotel neighborhood, eat where their concierge recommends, and take taxis when the subway station is 100 meters away. A modicum of geographic ambition — taking the 7 train to Flushing, the L train to Williamsburg, the A train to Washington Heights — changes the economics of the entire trip.
