New York City's geographic position on the northeastern US seaboard puts it within easy reach of some of the most varied landscapes on the East Coast. The Hamptons beach resort towns are 2 hours by train. The Hudson Valley wine region is 90 minutes north by car. Philadelphia's historic district is 90 minutes south by Amtrak. The Catskill Mountains begin 2.5 hours north.

Between matches, these options provide the kind of variation a week-long stay in a single city eventually requires.

---

## The Hamptons (East End of Long Island)

**Getting there**: Long Island Rail Road (LIRR) from Penn Station to Southampton or East Hampton. Journey time: 2–2.5 hours depending on which village. $20–30 each way. Alternatively, the Hampton Jitney bus from 40th Street and Lexington Avenue ($30–40 each way, approximately same journey time).

The Hamptons are a chain of resort towns on the South Fork of Long Island — Southampton, Water Mill, Bridgehampton, East Hampton, Amagansett, Montauk — stretching eastward toward the tip of Long Island at Montauk Point.

In June and July, this is where affluent New Yorkers go on weekends. The beaches are excellent (wide, Atlantic-facing, ocean swimming), the villages are attractive (historic shingled houses, main streets with boutiques and restaurants), and the food is surprisingly good for a resort area.

**The beaches**: Ocean beaches on the south side of the fork are Atlantic-facing — stronger surf, bigger waves, colder water than the protected bay beaches. **Main Beach** in East Hampton is the classic Hamptons beach. **Long Beach** in Sag Harbor is more sheltered and warmer.

**Montauk**: The easternmost town on the South Fork has a fishing port atmosphere entirely different from the manicured Southampton resort experience. The fish is fresh (the commercial fleet unloads here), the bars are more down-to-earth, and **Montauk Point Lighthouse** (the oldest lighthouse in New York State, 1797) is worth the eastern tip walk.

**Practical**: The Hamptons are extremely busy on summer weekends. Arriving Friday or Saturday means sharing the LIRR with thousands of city residents and paying peak rates for accommodation. A Tuesday or Wednesday visit is dramatically more peaceful.

## Hudson Valley

**Getting there**: Metro-North train from Grand Central Terminal to Cold Spring, Hudson, or Rhinecliff. Journey times: 1.5–2.5 hours depending on destination. $20–30 each way. Alternatively, 90 minutes–2 hours by car via the Taconic State Parkway or I-87.

The Hudson Valley is a 150-mile stretch of river, mountain, and agricultural land north of New York City that has seen significant investment in restaurants and culture over the past decade.

**Beacon**: A small city 1.5 hours from Grand Central (Metro-North) that contains the **Dia:Beacon** art museum — a massive 1929 Nabisco factory converted into exhibition space for the Dia Art Foundation's large-scale permanent collection (Serra, Judd, Heizer, Turrell). The museum alone justifies the trip. The town's Main Street has galleries and restaurants that reflect a neighborhood slowly gentrifying out of reach.

**Cold Spring**: A preserved 19th-century river town with excellent views of Storm King Mountain across the Hudson. Easy Amtrak/Metro-North access. Main Street has antique stores and a few good restaurants. The **Hudson Highlands** hiking trails begin here — the **Bull Hill** (Mount Taurus) hike to the summit above the Hudson is 2.5 hours round-trip and provides some of the most dramatic river views accessible by public transit from New York.

**Kingston and the Catskills**: Kingston is 2.5 hours north of Manhattan by car or Trailways bus — the starting point for exploring the Catskill Mountains. The Catskill Center towns (Woodstock, Phoenicia, Livingston Manor) have developed a food and accommodation scene that draws from [Brooklyn](/united-states/brooklyn-guide)'s restaurant culture. Woodstock was the location (in name) of the 1969 festival; the town itself is a pleasant artist community with galleries and good food.

## Philadelphia

**Getting there**: Amtrak from New York Penn Station to Philadelphia 30th Street Station. Journey time: 1 hour 15 minutes on Acela (premium), 1 hour 25 minutes on regular Northeast Regional. Fares: $20–45 each way on Regional; $70–120+ on Acela. Regular Regional service every 30–60 minutes.

Philadelphia is the fifth-largest city in the United States, the birthplace of American independence, and a city with a genuinely underrated food culture. It makes a compelling full-day trip from New York.

**Independence National Historical Park**: The Liberty Bell, Independence Hall, and the surrounding 18th-century streetscape are free to visit. The Liberty Bell Center has no admission fee. Timed entry tickets for Independence Hall tours are available (free, book ahead). The Constitution Center adjacent provides the historical context.

**The Philadelphia Museum of Art**: $25 general admission. The collection is extensive — Cézanne, van Gogh, Monet, a significant American collection. The Rocky steps are external and free.

**The Reading Terminal Market** (12th and Arch Streets): A covered market operating since 1893. The Amish vendors from Lancaster County sell pretzels, rotisserie chicken, scrapple, and shoofly pie. DiNic's roast pork sandwich is considered by many critics the best sandwich in America — the slow-roasted pork with provolone and sharp Italian greens on a long roll costs $12 and is extraordinary.

**The cheesesteak**: Philadelphia cheesesteaks are a legitimate regional food with a specific character (thinly sliced ribeye, Cheez Whiz or provolone, on a soft Amoroso roll). **Pat's King of Steaks** and **Geno's Steaks** (both at 9th and Passyunk, South Philly) are the famous rivals — both are pilgrimage sites that are decent rather than transcendent. **Jim's South Street** and **John's Roast Pork** are considered better by residents. Budget $12–16 for a cheesesteak.

## The Catskills (Longer Excursion)

**Getting there**: 2–2.5 hours by car via I-87 North to various exits. Bus via Trailways from Port Authority to Woodstock, Woodstock, or Fleischmanns (2.5–3 hours, $25–40 each way).

The Catskill Mountains are the traditional summer escape for New York City Jews and working-class families who came to the "Borscht Belt" resorts from the 1920s through the 1970s. Most of those resorts are gone; what remains is the landscape (Hudson Valley light, mountain swimming holes, dense hardwood forests) and a newer generation of restaurants and inns.

**Phoenicia**: A small town in the Catskills with Phoenicia Diner (a genuine 1960s diner with excellent updated food), hiking access to **Slide Mountain** (the highest peak in the Catskills), and swimming holes along the Esopus Creek. A full day trip that includes a diner breakfast, a creek swim, and a short hike is one of the best summer days accessible from New York.

**Woodstock**: Artist community, record stores, galleries, good coffee. The actual Woodstock Festival was held 45 miles away in Bethel (now the site of the Bethel Woods Center for the Arts).

## Fire Island National Seashore

**Getting there**: LIRR from Penn Station to Bay Shore (50 minutes, ~$14 each way), then ferry to Fire Island ($9.50–10 each way). Total: 75–90 minutes from Midtown.

Fire Island is a 32-mile barrier island off the south shore of Long Island with no cars (residents use wagons and bikes), white sand beaches, and boardwalk communities. It has two distinct sections: the **Pines** and **Cherry Grove** are the world-famous LGBTQ+ resort communities (especially prominent in summer); the western sections are family beach communities.

The **Fire Island National Seashore** wilderness area has virtually no facilities and no people — you walk the beach between the communities into an undeveloped Atlantic coastline. **Sailors Haven** has a free visitor center and the **Sunken Forest** — a maritime holly forest growing in the hollow between dunes, with a boardwalk trail.

The combination of ferry, boardwalk atmosphere, and empty ocean beach makes this one of the best summer day trips from New York.
