New York City has more restaurants than any other American city — approximately 27,000 at last count, across five boroughs and every cuisine imaginable. It also has a tourists-eating-badly problem: millions of visitors who spend $28 on a mediocre plate of pasta in Times Square, have a bad experience, and go home thinking New York food is overrated.

The food in New York is not overrated. It is exceptional, genuinely diverse, and in many categories (pizza, bagels, dim sum, Jewish deli, Dominican, Chinese) the best in the country. You just have to eat in the right places.

---

## Pizza

New York pizza is a distinct category. The style — large 18–20 inch pies with thin, foldable crust, tomato sauce, and mozzarella — was developed by Neapolitan immigrants in the early 20th century and then adapted over 120 years to something that is entirely its own. The by-the-slice model (cut from a larger pie, held in a vertical oven, reheated to order) is the everyday delivery mechanism.

**A proper slice costs $3–5**. If you're paying $8 for a slice, you are in a tourist-facing operation.

**The essential places**:

**Di Fara Pizza** (1424 Avenue J, Midwood, [Brooklyn](/united-states/brooklyn-guide)): Run by Domenico De Marco since 1965. He makes every pizza himself (slowly). The wait is 45–90 minutes. The pizza — a blend of three cheeses, San Marzano tomato, fresh basil snipped at the table — is worth every minute. Take the Q train to Avenue J.

**L'industrie Pizzeria** (254 S. 2nd Street, Williamsburg): Neapolitan technique with New York scale. The burrata-prosciutto slice is the signature. No wait because they sell faster than they pile up. $5–7/slice.

**Prince Street Pizza** (27 Prince Street, Nolita): The pepperoni pizza squares have an almost viral reputation for the quality of the crisped-cup pepperoni and the sauce-to-cheese ratio. Queue is typically 10–20 minutes. $5/square.

**Roberta's** (261 Moore Street, Bushwick, Brooklyn): The pizza that made Bushwick a destination. Wood-fired, Neapolitan-influenced, with creative topping combinations that work. The Bee Sting (mozzarella, tomato, soppressata, chili, honey) is the signature. Dinner reservations recommended; lunch is walk-in friendly.

**John's of Bleecker Street** (278 Bleecker Street, West Village): A 1929 institution. Whole pies only (no slices), coal-fired, thin-crust. The unreconstructed classic for when you want to understand the form.

## Bagels

The New York bagel argument — that water chemistry makes the difference — has been tested scientifically and is more about the boiling-before-baking technique, the specific flour, and 100+ years of refinement by Ashkenazi Jewish bakers. The result is a bagel with a chewy, shiny crust and a dense crumb that other cities genuinely cannot replicate.

**Ess-a-Bagel** (359 First Avenue, Murray Hill; 831 Third Avenue, Midtown East): The most no-nonsense operation. Large bagels, an extensive cream cheese menu ("schmear"), lox, whitefish, sable. Budget $12–18 for a full sandwich.

**Russ & Daughters** (179 E. Houston Street, Lower East Side): Operating since 1914, this is the apex of the Jewish appetizing tradition — smoked fish, caviar, herring, bagels, and babka. The smoked salmon on a bagel with cream cheese is the most historically significant breakfast in New York. Budget $20–25 for the full treatment.

**Absolute Bagels** (2788 Broadway, Upper West Side): The neighborhood institution that New Yorkers bring up when they want to win the best-bagel argument. Cash only, line out the door on weekend mornings, no seats. $2 for a plain bagel, $10–14 for a sandwich.

**Sadelle's** (463 West Broadway, SoHo): The upscale version — Nova Scotia salmon towers, premium cream cheeses, seated service. $30–40 per person. Worth it once for the full aesthetic experience.

## Dim Sum

New York's Chinatowns — in Manhattan and especially in Flushing, Queens — have the best dim sum outside of Hong Kong and the major Chinese cities.

**Flushing, Queens** (Main Street station, 7 train): The New World Mall Food Court and the Golden Shopping Mall basement are the entry points. The food here is regional Chinese — Sichuan malatang (spicy broth with vegetables and proteins), Xi'an hand-ripped noodles, Shanghainese xiaolongbao (soup dumplings), Cantonese roast meats — sold by individual vendors at $8–15 per dish. There are also full-service restaurants above ground level.

**The Joy Luck Palace** (135-20 39th Avenue, Flushing): A traditional dim sum hall with cart service on weekend mornings. Arrive by 11am or expect a 30-minute wait. Budget $20–25 per person for a full dim sum meal.

**Nom Wah Tea Parlor** (13 Doyers Street, Manhattan Chinatown): New York's oldest dim sum restaurant (1920). The atmosphere is irreplaceable — a 1940s-era tea parlor with vinyl booths. The har gow (shrimp dumplings) and turnip cake are the classics. Budget $25–35 per person.

## Dominican and Latin American Food

Washington Heights (Upper Manhattan, north of 155th Street) and the South Bronx are where the Dominican and Puerto Rican food culture of New York is at its most authentic and affordable.

**Malecon** (4141 Broadway, Washington Heights): The standard-bearer for Dominican food in the city. The roast chicken is the defining dish — slow-roasted, served with rice and beans and a vinegar-based Creole sauce. $16–18 for the whole chicken plate. The A train from Midtown to 168th Street takes 25 minutes.

**El Malecon** and the surrounding blocks on Broadway in Washington Heights have a concentration of Dominican restaurants, bodegas selling fresh juice, and bakeries with Dominican-style pastries that represent one of the most coherent neighborhood food cultures in the city.

**Louie & Ernie's Pizza** (1300 Crosby Avenue, the Bronx): Not Dominican but an entry point into Bronx Italian-American food culture. Neapolitan-influenced slices in a neighborhood that predates the food media industry.

## Jewish Deli

The Jewish delicatessen is an endangered species. The number of operational classic delis in New York has fallen from hundreds to dozens in 50 years. What remains is worth seeking out.

**Katz's Delicatessen** (205 E. Houston Street, Lower East Side): Open since 1888. The pastrami is brined, smoked, and steamed for five days, sliced thick, and served on rye with mustard. The sandwich costs $28 and weighs more than most meals. Tables are communal, the countermen are brusque, the overhead signs are genuinely funny. Lunch is busy; go at 11am or 2pm to avoid the peak queue.

**Barney Greengrass** (541 Amsterdam Avenue, Upper West Side): The Sturgeon King — a title self-assigned and fully deserved. The best smoked fish in the city. The Nova Scotia salmon with eggs and onions ($26–30) is a legitimate brunch dish.

**2nd Avenue Deli** (162 E. 33rd Street, Midtown; 1442 First Avenue, Upper East Side): The successor to a legendary deli that closed in 2006. The knishes, the matzo ball soup, and the pastrami sandwich are all excellent.

## Chinatown Specialties

Manhattan's Chinatown (south of Canal Street) is smaller than Flushing but more convenient and still has excellent specific operations.

**Lan Zhou Handmade Noodles** (144 E. Broadway): Hand-pulled noodles in beef broth for $8–10. One of the best cheap lunch options in the city.

**Tasty Dumpling** (54 Mulberry Street): Five pork-and-chive dumplings (pan-fried) for $3. This is the number that makes New York food work at a budget level.

**Joe's Shanghai** (9 Pell Street): The original Manhattan location of the xiao long bao (soup dumpling) institution. Use the provided straw to drain the soup carefully before eating. $12–14 for a basket of eight.

## The Outer Boroughs Worth Seeking

**Jackson Heights, Queens**: The most ethnically diverse commercial strip in the world. 74th Street and the surrounding blocks have Bangladeshi restaurants serving curry and biryani at $12–15, Indian sweet shops, Nepali restaurants, Ecuadorian cevicherias. The 7 train makes it 25 minutes from Times Square.

**Arthur Avenue, the Bronx** (also called the Real Little [Italy](/italy)): Butchers, cheese shops, pasta makers, and old-school Italian-American restaurants that haven't changed since the 1970s. The **Arthur Avenue Retail Market** is an indoor market with vendors selling bread, fresh pasta, and prepared foods. The surrounding restaurants — **Roberto's**, **Dominick's**, **Zero Otto Nove** — are the real thing. D train to Fordham Road plus a 15-minute walk or rideshare.

## Budget Eating

The floor of New York food is genuinely low if you know where to look:

- **Halal Guys** (53rd & 6th Avenue, the original cart): Chicken over rice with white sauce and hot sauce. $8–10. The white sauce is the reason this cart has spawned a franchise.
- **Tasty Dumpling** (5 for $3): As above.
- **Corner bodegas**: New York bodegas (small corner convenience stores) serve breakfast sandwiches — egg, cheese, and your choice of meat on a roll — for $4–6. This is the most culturally specific NYC breakfast experience and is excellent.
- **Chinatown**: $8–12 for any number of noodle dishes, dumplings, or rice plates
- **Pizza by the slice**: $3–5, everywhere, at all times

A genuine full-day food experience in New York — bodega breakfast sandwich, dim sum lunch in Flushing, katz's pastrami for an afternoon snack, and Dominican chicken for dinner — costs approximately $55–65 and represents the best of five different food traditions.
