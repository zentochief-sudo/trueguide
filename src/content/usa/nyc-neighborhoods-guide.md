New York City is five boroughs — Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, the Bronx, and Staten Island — with a collective population of 8.3 million. For World Cup visitors, the practical universe is primarily Manhattan and Brooklyn, with Queens offering some of the best food experiences and value accommodation in the metro area. Understanding the geography before you arrive determines whether you spend your trip comfortably or confused.

The organizing principle: MetLife Stadium is in New Jersey, connected by NJ Transit from Penn Station at 34th Street in Midtown Manhattan. Where you sleep should balance proximity to Penn Station with the kind of neighborhood experience you want.

---

## Midtown Manhattan

Midtown is the city's commercial core and the area most visitors default to — Penn Station is here, Grand Central Terminal is here, Times Square is here. The advantages are real: transit access is unsurpassed, hotels are plentiful, everything is open late. The disadvantage: Midtown is a city of office towers, chain restaurants, and tourist traps, and it bears less resemblance to the neighborhoods that make New York worth visiting than any other area.

**Hell's Kitchen** (West 40s–50s, 8th to 11th Avenues): The best subdistrict within Midtown for actual neighborhood life. Restaurant Row on 46th Street has been a theater-district dining institution for decades. The blocks around 9th Avenue have excellent ethnic restaurants — Portuguese, Korean, Brazilian — at mid-range prices. Walking distance to Penn Station.

**Murray Hill** (East 30s): Younger, more residential than Midtown proper. The blocks around Park Avenue South have good restaurants that aren't primarily tourist-facing. Adjacent to Penn Station (short walk or one subway stop).

**To stay in Midtown**: $280–500/night for most hotels in summer, higher during match weeks. **The Pod Hotels** (39th St and 42nd St) offer genuinely small rooms for $150–200/night — efficient use of space, practical, central.

## Upper West Side and Upper East Side

The residential neighborhoods along Central Park — the Upper West Side (west of the park, 59th–110th Streets) and Upper East Side (east of the park, same range) — offer a quieter, more local experience than Midtown while remaining well-connected to Penn Station by subway.

**Upper West Side**: The **1/2/3** trains run through it. Broadway from 72nd to 96th Street is a neighborhood shopping corridor with independent bookstores, diners, and the occasional serious restaurant. **Barney Greengrass** (541 Amsterdam Avenue) has been serving smoked fish since 1908. The **American Museum of Natural History** is here, as are the 72nd Street entrance to Central Park and the **Shakespearean Garden** in the park itself.

**Upper East Side**: Museum Mile runs along 5th Avenue from 82nd to 105th Streets — eight museums in 23 blocks, including the Metropolitan Museum of Art and the Guggenheim. More residential and quieter than the West Side. The **4/5/6 train** runs along Lexington Avenue.

**To stay**: $220–380/night for mid-range hotels in both areas. Often 20–30% cheaper than equivalent Midtown hotels.

## Greenwich Village and the West Village

The most photographed residential streets in Manhattan — low-brownstone blocks, irregular street grid, the sense that someone preserved an actual human-scale neighborhood by accident when the grid came north.

**Washington Square Park** is the center: dominated by NYU students, chess players, drug activity in one corner, and impromptu musical performances at all times. The arch is a proper monument. The park is fully operational and worth 30 minutes.

**The food**: The blocks around Bleecker Street (the West Village section) have the city's highest concentration of small, independent restaurants that aren't in guidebooks yet. **Via Carota** (51 Grove Street) is arguably the best Italian restaurant in Manhattan and requires a reservation or willingness to wait at the bar. **Superiority Burger** (9 Avenue A, now in the East Village) serves meatless burgers that are genuinely extraordinary.

**To stay**: Limited hotels; mostly boutique options at $280–450/night. The **The Standard, East Village** and similar properties anchor this tier.

## SoHo and Nolita

SoHo (South of Houston Street) is New York's shopping district — cast-iron architecture converted into retail, galleries that have become Nike stores, and the particular phenomenon of beautiful buildings full of things you don't need. Worth walking through for the architecture; don't expect local restaurants to be affordable.

**Nolita** (North of Little Italy) is more interesting: a small grid of streets between SoHo and the Lower East Side with some of the best restaurants in the city. **Prince Street Pizza** (27 Prince Street) is the essential stop — the pepperoni pizza squares have a particular crispiness that justifies the queue. **Uncle Boons** for Thai food.

## Lower East Side

The Lower East Side was the center of Jewish immigrant New York in the early 20th century and has become one of the city's most interesting food-and-nightlife neighborhoods.

**Katz's Delicatessen** (205 E. Houston Street): Open since 1888. The pastrami sandwich costs $28 and feeds two people sharing. The ritual: get a ticket at the door, order at the counter, eat at a table, don't lose the ticket. The line moves fast at lunch but the experience is worth 45 minutes of your time.

**The Essex Market** (88 Essex Street): A modernized market hall with vendors selling everything from Puerto Rican mofongo to Korean banchan.

**Nightlife**: The LES has the densest concentration of bars per block of any neighborhood in Manhattan. **Happy Ending** (300 Broome Street, in a former massage parlor), **The Magician**, **Welcome to the Johnsons** — these are local bars, not cocktail-bar experiences.

## Williamsburg, Brooklyn

Cross the Williamsburg Bridge from the Lower East Side (15-minute walk or the M train from Delancey Street) and you're in Brooklyn's most visited neighborhood — and its most unfairly dismissed.

Williamsburg became famous for being cool in the 2000s, then became famous for being overrun, then quietly became a genuinely good place to eat and drink. **Lilia** (567 Union Avenue) makes the best pasta in New York City and requires a reservation two weeks ahead. **L'industrie Pizzeria** (254 S. 2nd Street) makes the best Neapolitan-influenced pizza in Brooklyn. **Marlow & Sons** (81 Broadway) has been a local bar and oyster institution for 20 years.

The **L train** connects Williamsburg to Union Square in 7 minutes, and Penn Station is accessible from there via the 1/2/3 trains. Total: 25–30 minutes to Penn Station.

**To stay**: $170–280/night for hotels. **The William Vale** (111 N. 12th Street) has the best rooftop pool in the borough with views of the Manhattan skyline across the East River.

## DUMBO and Brooklyn Heights

DUMBO (Down Under the Manhattan Bridge Overpass) is the neighborhood that produced the most photographed view in New York: the gap between two apartment buildings framing the Manhattan Bridge, with the Brooklyn Bridge visible beyond it. Worth the photograph. The neighborhood has become very expensive to eat and drink in.

**Brooklyn Heights Promenade** is the accessible public esplanade along the Brooklyn waterfront — a flat, 1-mile walkway with unobstructed views of Lower Manhattan. Sunset from here, with the office towers lit up gold, is a proper New York experience.

**Brooklyn Bridge Park** extends along the waterfront below Brooklyn Heights, with piers converted to sports fields, picnic areas, and the **Jane's Carousel** (a restored 1922 carousel). Free entry. The park connects Dumbo and Brooklyn Heights to Red Hook.

## Queens: Flushing and Jackson Heights

**Flushing** (accessible by 7 train, 45 minutes from Midtown): The second-largest Chinatown in America, with a food culture that makes Manhattan's Chinatown look preliminary. The **New World Mall Food Court** and **Golden Shopping Mall** have dozens of vendors serving specific regional Chinese dishes — Sichuan malatang, Shanghainese soup dumplings, Xi'an hand-ripped noodles — at $8–14 per dish. This is where New York Chinese food geeks eat.

**Jackson Heights** (7 train or E/F/M/R train, 30 minutes from Midtown): The most ethnically diverse neighborhood in the world by some measures — Bangladeshi, Indian, Nepali, Colombian, Ecuadorian, Mexican restaurants within a few blocks. The Indian and South Asian food here (particularly the Bangladeshi restaurants on 73rd Street) is the best in New York.

**Hotel value**: Queens hotels run $130–200/night — the best accommodation value in the metro area. Long Island City (directly across from Midtown) has several newer hotels at $150–240/night that offer a straight shot to Penn Station on the 7 train.

## The Bronx

The Bronx is the most undervisited borough by international tourists and has two specific claims on World Cup visitors' time.

**The Bronx is the birthplace of hip-hop** (specifically the South Bronx). The **Universal Hip Hop Museum** (opening fully in 2026) is the new anchor for this history. The **Yankee Stadium** neighborhood has a food scene centered on Dominican, Puerto Rican, and West African cuisine that is unlike anything in Manhattan.

**Arthur Avenue** (the Bronx's Italian market district) is the Italian food destination — not Little Italy in Manhattan, which has become a tourist trap. Arthur Avenue's butchers, pasta makers, cheese importers, and bakeries have been operating since Italian immigrants settled here in the early 20th century. Get the Calabrese bread and the fresh mozzarella and eat on the steps.

## Neighborhood Summary for World Cup Visitors

**Best transit to MetLife**: Midtown (Penn Station walking distance)  
**Best value for money**: Queens (Long Island City, Flushing), Brooklyn (Williamsburg)  
**Best food neighborhoods**: LES, West Village, Williamsburg, Flushing (Queens)  
**Best for atmosphere**: West Village, Brooklyn Heights, High Line area  
**Best for nightlife**: LES, East Village, Williamsburg
