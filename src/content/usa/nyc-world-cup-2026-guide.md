New York City is hosting FIFA World Cup 2026 matches at [MetLife Stadium](/united-states/metlife-stadium-guide) in East Rutherford, New Jersey — seven miles west of Midtown Manhattan across the Hudson River. The stadium is technically in a different state, but New York City functions as the hub: it has the hotels, the airports, the restaurants, the nightlife, and the particular energy that makes hosting a World Cup in this city different from hosting it anywhere else on earth.

The city is dense, loud, expensive, and endlessly varied. Its neighborhoods are distinct enough to feel like separate cities. Its subway runs 24 hours. The pizza is better than it has any right to be and the bagels are unreasonably superior to those made anywhere else. This is where the World Cup's American chapter gets written.

## MetLife Stadium and Getting There

**MetLife Stadium** is located in East Rutherford, New Jersey, 8.5 miles west of Midtown Manhattan. Its standard capacity is 82,500, with temporary FIFA expansion configurations potentially reaching 87,000–92,000 for the tournament. It opened in 2010 and hosts both the New York Giants and New York Jets (neither of whom own it — it's operated by a joint venture). The Super Bowl has been held here. The first and last matches of World Cup 2026 — including the Final — will be played here.

**Transit options**:

The **NJ Transit train** from Penn Station (34th Street) is the primary option. The Meadowlands Sports Complex station is a direct connection to the stadium — no subway transfers required. Walk from most Midtown hotels to Penn Station (or take any train to 34th Street-Penn Station), buy an NJ Transit ticket ($7–10 each way), and ride 20–25 minutes to the stadium.

The **Port Authority Bus Terminal** (41st Street and 8th Avenue) runs express buses on match days directly to MetLife. Journey time approximately 30–40 minutes.

**Rideshare**: Surge pricing on match days will push Uber/Lyft from Midtown to $60–110 each way. If you must rideshare, prebook or leave very early.

**Driving**: Parking at MetLife costs $40–75 pre-purchased. Post-match traffic around the sports complex can extend a 30-minute drive to 90 minutes. The NJ Transit train is faster.

**The key advice**: Buy NJ Transit tickets in advance via the NJ Transit app. On match days, the ticket queues at Penn Station can be 20–30 minutes long. The app eliminates this entirely.

## Airports

New York is served by three major airports:

**JFK Airport** (Queens): The primary international gateway. Accessible by subway (A train or AirTrain + Jamaica Station) to Midtown in 60–75 minutes, or rideshare ($45–70 base). Book the AirTrain ($8.50) + subway ($2.90) combination — it's slower but dramatically cheaper and reliable.

**Newark Liberty Airport (EWR)** (New Jersey): Closer to MetLife Stadium than JFK. AirTrain from the airport to Newark Penn Station, then NJ Transit to New York Penn Station. Total journey to Midtown: 45–60 minutes. The PATH train from Newark Penn to 33rd Street in Manhattan: $2.75. Often the best combination for match-day arrivals.

**LaGuardia Airport (LGA)** (Queens): No direct train connection. Bus (Q70 to Jackson Heights subway) or rideshare ($25–45 to Midtown under normal conditions). Budget 45–75 minutes.

## Where to Stay

New York City's neighborhoods function as distinct sub-cities. Your choice determines which subway lines you live on and how your non-match days organize themselves.

**Midtown (34th–59th Streets)**: The default for first-time visitors and the most practical for MetLife access — Penn Station is right here. Hotels run $250–500/night in summer, more during World Cup match weeks. Times Square is the center of this area and not worth dwelling in. The value is proximity to transit, not the neighborhood itself.

**Hell's Kitchen (40s–50s, west of 8th Avenue)**: Better than Midtown proper. More restaurants, more diverse, still walking distance to Penn Station. Hotels $200–380/night.

**Midtown East (34th–59th, east of 5th Avenue)**: Near Grand Central Terminal and the 4/5/6 subway lines. Quiet by Manhattan standards. Hotels $220–400/night.

**Chelsea and the West Village**: A bus or short subway to Penn Station. Better neighborhoods for food and street life than Midtown. Hotels $250–420/night.

**[Brooklyn](/united-states/brooklyn-guide) (Williamsburg, DUMBO, Park Slope)**: 20–30 minutes to Penn Station by L train or F train. $30–100 cheaper per night than equivalent Manhattan hotels. Excellent food neighborhoods. Increasingly where savvy World Cup visitors will stay.

**Long Island City (Queens)**: 15 minutes to Midtown by 7 train. Some of the best hotel value in the metropolitan area at $160–250/night. Less tourist infrastructure but good food options.

## The Subway

New York's subway is the single most important piece of visitor infrastructure and the thing most visitors either fear unnecessarily or misuse.

**The basics**: Unlimited-ride cards are available as 7-day passes ($34) on the OMNY system (tap with contactless card, Apple Pay, or Google Pay) or via a MetroCard. Single rides are $2.90. The 7-day unlimited is the obvious choice for any stay of three or more days.

**Key lines for World Cup visitors**:
- **1/2/3** (red): Runs north-south through the West Side, stops at Penn Station (34th St)
- **A/C/E** (blue): Connects JFK, Penn Station (34th), Times Square
- **4/5/6** (green): East Side north-south, connects to Grand Central
- **L** (grey): Connects Williamsburg (Brooklyn) to Union Square and Chelsea
- **N/Q/R/W** (yellow): Connects Brooklyn, Midtown, and Queens; Rockefeller Center stop useful

The subway runs 24 hours. It is less clean and less reliable at 3am than at 3pm, but it runs. This makes it possible to have genuine late nights in New York in a way that is impossible in Los Angeles.

**For MetLife**: Take any train to 34th Street-Penn Station, buy NJ Transit tickets (app preferred), board the Meadowlands-bound train. Do this with 90 minutes to spare for match days.

## June and July in NYC

Heat and humidity. June averages 25–28°C with high humidity; July averages 28–32°C with heat index values often reaching 36–40°C in the city's urban heat island. The humidity is significant — if you are from a dry climate, this will feel more extreme than the numbers suggest.

Thunderstorms are common in July. They arrive quickly, dump significant rain, and typically clear within an hour. Carry a compact umbrella or use the NYC street phenomenon of $5 umbrella vendors who appear at every intersection the moment it starts raining.

The compensating factor: New York in summer is its most alive version. Outdoor concerts in Central Park (SummerStage is a free series), rooftop bars until 4am, the full summer programming at Brooklyn Bridge Park, open-air movies, and the particular energy of a city that treats summer as a release from winter constraints.

## Food Strategy

New York has the most diverse restaurant ecosystem in North America. The strategic mistake is eating in Times Square or the Midtown tourist corridor.

**What New York does best**:
- **Pizza**: New York-style thin-crust by-the-slice. $3–5/slice. The best operations are in Brooklyn and the outer boroughs. **Di Fara** in Midwood, **L'industrie** in Williamsburg, **Prince Street Pizza** in Nolita.
- **Bagels**: The best in the world. The water argument is real but also reflects 100+ years of bagel-making tradition. **Ess-a-Bagel**, **Russ & Daughters**, **Absolute Bagels**.
- **Dim sum**: Chinatown in Manhattan and especially Flushing (Queens) — the best outside Hong Kong.
- **Dominican and Puerto Rican food**: The Bronx and Washington Heights have the best versions in the country.
- **Jewish deli**: An endangered species but Katz's Delicatessen (205 E. Houston St) has survived since 1888.

**Budget**: New York is expensive, but the by-the-slice pizza, dollar dumplings in Flushing, and food cart halal rice-and-chicken ($8) create a genuine affordable-eating tier.

## Between Match Days

Central Park is 843 acres in the middle of Manhattan. The **Ramble** (a wooded area in the center), **Bethesda Terrace**, and the reservoir loop are the parts worth finding — not the tourist-concentrated southern end.

The **High Line** is an elevated rail-turned-park running 1.45 miles through Chelsea and Hudson Yards. The views of the Hudson and the street life below are excellent.

**Brooklyn Bridge**: Walk it eastbound from the Manhattan side in the morning for the best light on the skyline behind you. Allow 30–40 minutes each way.

**The Metropolitan Museum of Art**: $30 suggested admission. The Egyptian wing, Greek and Roman antiquities, and European paintings collections each warrant a full afternoon. Plan for 3–4 hours minimum.

## Realistic Budget

New York is the most expensive World Cup city on the American roster. Budget travelers can manage $130–160/day (hostel, street food and by-the-slice, subway). A mid-range trip with a proper hotel, real restaurant meals, and occasional taxis runs $320–420/day. Hotel rates on match-week dates will be 50–80% above normal summer rates. Book early, prioritize Brooklyn or Long Island City for value, and treat the $2.90 subway as the infrastructure that makes everything work.
