The Philadelphia cheesesteak is one of the few American regional foods that has an actual origin story, a specific required ingredient, and a set of authenticity debates that locals take seriously. For World Cup visitors, understanding the format before arriving makes the experience of eating one significantly better than walking up to a counter without context.

---

## What It Is

A cheesesteak is thinly sliced ribeye beef cooked on a flat griddle, mixed with optional sautéed onions, and served in an Amoroso hoagie roll with one of three cheese options: Cheez Whiz, provolone, or American. That's it.

**The components in detail**:

**The meat**: Ribeye is standard — its fat content keeps the thin-sliced beef moist on the griddle. Some shops use other cuts; ribeye is the benchmark.

**The roll**: The Amoroso Baking Company supplies rolls to most Philadelphia cheesesteak shops. The roll is softer than a baguette, with a slightly crisp exterior that absorbs the beef juices without disintegrating. Substituting a different roll produces a different sandwich; the Amoroso roll is part of the cheesesteak's specific character.

**The cheese options**:
- **Cheez Whiz**: The processed cheese sauce invented by Kraft in 1952. Liquid at room temperature; poured over the beef. The most traditional choice at Pat's and Geno's. The question "should a good sandwich use Cheez Whiz?" is not relevant in this context — the Whiz is part of the specific flavor equation.
- **Provolone**: Sliced provolone, mild, melted over the beef. The choice of people who want cheese without the processed element.
- **American**: Processed American cheese slices. The middle option.

**The onion question**: Wit (with sautéed onions) or wit-out (without). Sautéed onions are standard in most shops.

---

## How to Order

Walk up to the counter and say your order in the correct format. At Pat's and Geno's, the order structure is: [cheese] wit or wit-out.

- "Whiz wit" = Cheez Whiz with onions
- "Provolone wit-out" = provolone without onions
- "American wit" = American cheese with onions

First-timers should order Whiz wit to taste the traditional format.

**At other shops**: Many Philadelphia cheesesteak places use a more normal order format. The Pat's/Geno's ordering code is specific to those two operations; elsewhere you can just say what you want.

## Pat's King of Steaks vs. Geno's Steaks

**Pat's King of Steaks** (1237 E Passyunk Avenue, South Philadelphia)

Pat Olivieri invented the cheesesteak in 1930 (origin story: he improvised a sandwich at his hot dog cart; a cab driver smelled it and asked for one; the sandwich spread from there). Pat's has operated at this corner continuously since then, now run by the fourth generation. Cash only. Open 24 hours.

**Geno's Steaks** (1219 S 9th Street, directly across the intersection)

Joey Vento opened Geno's in 1966 to compete with Pat's — in full view of the original across Passyunk Avenue. Geno's has a more theatrical exterior (flashing lights, neon signs) and is equally well-known. Cash only.

**The reality**: The two sandwiches are extremely similar. Locals have preferences, but objectively the quality gap between them is minimal. The experience of ordering at both in one visit (15 minutes; they're across the street from each other) is the tourist version of the Pat's vs. Geno's debate, and it's a legitimate way to form an opinion.

## Beyond Pat's and Geno's: The Better Cheesesteaks

**Dalessandro's Steaks** (600 Wendover Street, Roxborough)

Most Philadelphians who pay attention to food cite Dalessandro's as the superior cheesesteak — thicker bread, better quality beef, and a neighborhood atmosphere without the tourist setup. Open since 1960. Cash only. Requires a rideshare or car to reach (not in the tourist circuit).

**John's Roast Pork** (14 E Snyder Avenue, South Philly)

A James Beard Award Classic winner — their cheesesteak is excellent, but the **roast pork sandwich** (slow-roasted pork, sharp provolone, broccoli rabe) may be even better. A short detour from the Pat's/Geno's area. Cash only; closes in the early afternoon.

**Jim's Steaks** (400 South Street)

A Center City-accessible option — on the corner of South and 4th Streets, closer to Old City than Geno's or Pat's. A reliable option for visitors staying near Old City who don't want to travel to South Philadelphia.

**Steve's Prince of Steaks** (7200 Bustleton Avenue, Northeast Philly)

A favorite in northeast Philadelphia with a cult following — requires a car or long rideshare, but people who've been there rank it as one of the best.

## The Roast Pork Sandwich

Philadelphians who know food will tell you the roast pork sandwich is better than the cheesesteak. It is: slow-roasted pork (typically pork shoulder or loin), sharp provolone, and broccoli rabe (bitter Italian greens sautéed in garlic), on an Amoroso roll.

The combination of the pork's richness, the sharpness of the provolone, and the slight bitterness of the broccoli rabe is more complex than a cheesesteak. **Tommy DiNic's** at Reading Terminal Market is the most accessible version — a National competition winner as best sandwich in the US.

## Practical Notes

**Cash only**: Pat's, Geno's, John's, and Dalessandro's are cash-only. ATMs are nearby.

**Hours**: Pat's and Geno's are 24 hours; most other shops close at 10pm–midnight.

**Condiments**: Hot peppers and hot sauce are available at most shops. Ketchup is considered an incorrect condiment; mustard is occasionally available.

**The cheesesteak pizza and other derivatives**: Available at many pizza shops. Not the traditional format but legitimate in their own right.
