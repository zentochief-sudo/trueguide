Philadelphia's food reputation internationally begins and ends with the cheesesteak — which is worth eating but barely represents the city's food culture. The more honest picture includes Reading Terminal Market (one of the best covered markets in the US), the 9th Street Italian Market (operating since 1915), a James Beard Award-winning Israeli restaurant that has changed how American food culture thinks about Middle Eastern cooking, and a Fishtown restaurant scene that has produced chefs who now have national reputations. Philadelphia is the most underrated food city in the US.

---

## The Cheesesteak

The cheesesteak is real and worth eating — thinly sliced ribeye cooked on a flat-top griddle with onions (or without), served on an Amoroso hoagie roll with Cheez Whiz, provolone, or American cheese. The format originated in Philadelphia in the 1930s.

**The Pat's vs. Geno's question**: Pat's King of Steaks (1237 E Passyunk Avenue) and Geno's Steaks (1219 S 9th Street) sit across the intersection from each other in South Philadelphia — the most famous cheesesteak rivalry in the city. Locals are divided; tourists make it a pilgrimage. The reality: both serve legitimate cheesesteaks; the quality difference between them is minimal; the tourist experience of ordering at both within 20 minutes is the actual activity.

**Ordering**: "Whiz wit" means Cheez Whiz with onions; "Whiz wit-out" is without onions. This is the ordering code you need to know.

**Price**: $13–17 for a full cheesesteak.

**Beyond Pat's and Geno's**: 
- **Dalessandro's** (600 Wendover Street, Roxborough): The steak most Philadelphians will cite when tourists aren't around. Thicker slices, superior roll, neighborhood institution since 1960. Less accessible (requires a car or rideshare) but worth it.
- **John's Roast Pork** (14 E Snyder Avenue, South Philly): A James Beard Award winner in the Classic category — the roast pork sandwich (with sharp provolone and broccoli rabe) is arguably better than their cheesesteak, and the cheesesteak is still excellent.

## Reading Terminal Market

**12th and Arch Streets, Center City** | Open daily 8am–6pm

Reading Terminal Market has operated since 1892 in a former train shed — a covered market with 80+ vendors selling prepared food, groceries, meat, fish, cheese, and specialty items. The most useful food experience in Philadelphia for visitors who want variety in one place.

**The specific vendors**:
- **Beiler's Donuts**: Amish donuts from a Lancaster County family vendor — the most popular item in the market. $1.50–2.50 each, lines by 9am.
- **DiNic's Roast Pork**: Famous for the roast pork sandwich (Bon Appétit named it the best sandwich in America). Slow-roasted pork, sharp provolone, broccoli rabe. $12.
- **Hershel's East Side Deli**: New York-style Jewish deli in Philadelphia — pastrami, corned beef, matzo ball soup. The most complete deli menu outside New York.
- **Federal Donuts**: Fried chicken and donuts. The fried chicken here is among the best in the city.
- **Down Home Diner**: Soul food in the market — grits, fried chicken, eggs, and the specific comfort food tradition of the African American community.

## The Italian Market

**9th Street between Wharton and Fitzwater Streets, South Philadelphia** | Tuesday–Saturday

The 9th Street Italian Market is the oldest outdoor market in the US (since 1915) and one of the most active. The original Italian vendors have been joined by Mexican, Vietnamese, and other immigrant communities — the market reflects the successive waves of South Philadelphia immigration.

**The food vendors**:
- **DiBruno Bros.** (930 S 9th Street): The best cheese shop in Philadelphia — Italian imports alongside domestic artisan cheeses. The prepared foods counter has excellent Italian salumi.
- **Claudio's** (926 S 9th Street): Specialty Italian imports — pastas, olive oils, anchovies, and the products of the Italian grocery trade.
- **Giordano's** (1020 S 9th Street): The cheesesteak at the Italian Market — in the heart of the Italian community rather than at the Pat's/Geno's corner.

## Top Restaurants

**Zahav** (237 St. James Place, Old City): The most acclaimed restaurant in Philadelphia — Michael Solomonov's modern Israeli cooking using Palestinian, Yemenite, and Eastern Mediterranean flavors. The hummus program alone has changed how American restaurants approach the dish. The salatim (the opening array of small salads and spreads), the whole lamb shoulder (ordered in advance), and the long laffa flatbread are the reference items. Dinner: $70–90 per person. Reservations required months in advance.

**Vernick Food & Drink** (2031 Walnut Street, Rittenhouse): Greg Vernick's American cooking with a whole-animal, seasonal approach — one of the most consistent restaurants in Philadelphia for the past decade. The charcuterie, the cellar program, and the bar menu are the day-to-day anchors. Dinner: $60–80 per person.

**Vetri Cucina** (1312 Spruce Street, Rittenhouse): Marc Vetri's Italian fine dining — the most technically accomplished Italian restaurant in Philadelphia, known for house-made pasta and whole-animal butchery. The tasting menu: $155/person.

**Suraya** (1528 Frankford Avenue, Fishtown): Lebanese cooking in a Fishtown setting — the mezze selection, the charcoal-grilled meats, and the baklava program are the best Lebanese cooking in the city. Dinner: $55–70 per person.

## Breakfast and Bakeries

**Koch's Deli** (4309 Locust Street): A Philadelphia institution for deli sandwiches and breakfast. The pastrami egg sandwich is the specific item.

**High Street on Market** (308 Market Street, Old City): A bakery and all-day café with outstanding bread (the Everything Bread is a reference item) and breakfast. $12–18.

**Gruyère** (multiple locations): A cheese and wine shop with an attached café — the cheese plate and coffee in the morning is the counter-intuitive recommendation.
