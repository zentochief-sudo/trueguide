Philadelphia has a claim on American history that no other World Cup host city can match: the Declaration of Independence was signed here in 1776; the Constitution of the United States was written and signed here in 1787; Benjamin Franklin lived, worked, and died here; and the city served as the nation's capital from 1790 to 1800. For World Cup visitors from outside the US, Independence National Historical Park provides a walking tour of the events that created the country hosting the tournament.

## Independence National Historical Park

The park comprises 20+ sites within a 1.5-mile radius in Old City Philadelphia. Most can be covered on foot in a full day; the essential sites take 3–4 hours.

**Independence Hall** (520 Chestnut Street)

The single most significant building in American history. Built in 1732 as the Pennsylvania State House, it was the meeting place of the Second Continental Congress (1775–1781) and the Constitutional Convention (1787).

- **July 4, 1776**: The Declaration of Independence was adopted here, formally announcing the separation of the 13 colonies from Britain.
- **1787**: The Constitutional Convention met here from May to September. The US Constitution was signed in this room on September 17, 1787.
- **The Assembly Room**: The room where both documents were signed — preserved largely as it appeared, with the rising sun chair used by George Washington as Convention president. This is the most significant room in American political history.

**Tours**: Timed-entry tickets required; $1 advance reservation at recreation.gov. Tours run hourly; 30 minutes per tour. Book in advance — tours fill during summer.

**The Liberty Bell Center** (526 Market Street)

The Liberty Bell is a copper and tin bell cast in 1752, used to summon lawmakers to sessions in Independence Hall. It cracked — twice — and was eventually removed from service. The bell became a symbol for the abolitionist movement in the 19th century (the term "Liberty Bell" was given by abolitionists, not colonists).

The bell is housed in a glass pavilion with Independence Hall visible directly behind it through the window — a deliberate framing that connects the object to the building where it rang. Free entry.

**The crack**: The bell's famous crack runs from the rim to the crown. It cracked on the first test ring after arriving from London (1752), was recast, cracked again in 1846 while ringing for George Washington's birthday, and was taken out of service.

**Franklin Court** (between Market and Chestnut Streets, near 3rd Street)

Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790) lived in a house at this location from 1763 until his death. The house no longer exists; the National Park Service built a ghostly steel frame ("ghost structure") indicating the dimensions of the original house. Underground, the Franklin museum examines his life as printer, inventor, scientist, diplomat, and political thinker.

Franklin's specific inventions: the lightning rod (which he refused to patent), bifocal glasses, the Franklin stove. His political role: Pennsylvania's representative to London in the years before the Revolution; ambassador to France during the Revolution (where he secured French military support that was essential to the American victory); delegate to the Constitutional Convention.

**Carpenters' Hall** (320 Chestnut Street)

The First Continental Congress met here in 1774 — a meeting of colonial representatives to discuss British taxation without representation, one year before the Revolution began. The building belongs to the Carpenters' Company of Philadelphia (the oldest builders' guild in the US, founded 1724), which still meets here. Free, open to visitors.

**Christ Church** (20 N American Street)

The Anglican church founded in 1695. George Washington, John Adams, Benjamin Franklin, and most of the Philadelphia signers of the Declaration attended services here. The original boxed pews are in place; a historical marker in pew 70 indicates where Washington sat. Still an active Episcopal parish; open for tours. Free.

**Elfreth's Alley** (between 2nd and Front Streets, north of Arch Street)

The oldest continuously inhabited residential street in the United States — 32 houses built between 1703 and 1836, all still occupied. Two houses open as museums ($5); the street itself is public and the architecture of urban colonial Philadelphia is visible as built. Walking through is one of the best time-travel experiences in American historical preservation.

## The Barnes Foundation

**2025 Benjamin Franklin Parkway** | $30

Not American Revolution history — but an extraordinary museum that requires explanation for international visitors who may not know it.

Albert C. Barnes (1872–1951) was a Philadelphia pharmacist who became rich and spent the money buying Impressionist and Post-Impressionist paintings in Paris in the 1910s and 1920s — buying directly from Matisse, Picasso, Cézanne, and their peers, before these artists were universally recognized. He assembled one of the greatest collections of Impressionist art in the world (181 Renoirs, 69 Cézannes, 59 Matisses, 46 Picassos).

Barnes designed the hanging to juxtapose paintings by different artists, periods, and movements in ways that force comparison. The original "ensemble" arrangements are preserved in the current building. The collection includes more Renoirs than the Musée d'Orsay and more Cézannes than any single museum.

## Eastern State Penitentiary

**2027 Fairmount Avenue** | $20

A Gothic Revival prison built in 1829 — the first penitentiary in the world designed on the "Pennsylvania system" of solitary confinement and individual reflection. Al Capone was incarcerated here in 1929.

The prison was closed in 1971 and opened as a historic site. Much of it remains in its post-closure deteriorated state — crumbling vaulted corridors, cell blocks open to the sky. The "ruin aesthetic" is intentional. One of the most interesting and atmospheric historic sites in Philadelphia.
