San Francisco is a 7-by-7-mile peninsula at the mouth of the Bay — one of the most geographically distinctive American cities, built on steep hills that create dramatic views of the water from almost every neighborhood. It is also genuinely unusual: a city shaped by multiple layers of history (the Gold Rush, the counterculture, the tech industry) that have each left visible residue in the neighborhoods, the architecture, and the culture.

For World Cup visitors, San Francisco is the base city for Levi's Stadium matches, but it is more than that — it is a city that merits 2–3 days of independent exploration.

## The Essential Landmarks

**The Golden Gate Bridge**

The 1937 suspension bridge connecting San Francisco to Marin County — the most photographed structure in the United States. At 2.7 miles, it is walkable across (allow 90 minutes round-trip). The most dramatic views are from:
- **Battery Spencer** (Marin Headlands side): A 15-minute drive from the bridge. The headlands viewpoint looks back at the bridge with the SF skyline behind it. Worth the drive.
- **Fort Point** (under the bridge, SF side): Inside the Golden Gate National Recreation Area. Walking directly under the span from below.
- **Baker Beach**: A beach on the SF side with direct bridge views, 15 minutes from the bridge parking area.

**Alcatraz**

The former federal penitentiary on an island in the Bay — active 1934–1963, housing Al Capone and other high-profile prisoners. The ferry from Pier 33 takes 15 minutes. The audio tour narrated by former guards and inmates is excellent — genuinely more interesting than most museum experiences.

**Booking**: Alcatraz tickets sell out 2–3 weeks in advance during summer. Book at alcatrazcruises.com before arrival. Day and evening tours available; the evening tour is worth the slight premium for the sunset lighting.

**Cost**: $45 including ferry.

**Fisherman's Wharf and Pier 39**

The tourist waterfront — crowded, commercial, but the Dungeness crab (in season), the sea lion colony at Pier 39 (permanent residents, fascinating to watch), and the view across the Bay are genuine draws. The best food here is Buena Vista Café's Irish Coffee (2765 Hyde Street) and the crab vendors on the pier itself. Manage expectations: this is the tourist version of SF.

## Neighborhoods to Walk

**The Haight-Ashbury**

The neighborhood where the 1960s counterculture happened — the Summer of Love (1967), the Grateful Dead's house at 710 Ashbury, Janis Joplin's apartment at 635 Ashbury. Upper Haight is now gentrified vintage clothing and independent shops; Lower Haight is more neighborhood-oriented. The history is real; the current scene is more subdued than the mythology suggests.

**The Castro**

San Francisco's LGBTQ+ neighborhood since the 1970s — Harvey Milk's camera store at 575 Castro, the Castro Theatre (a preserved 1922 movie palace that still shows films), the bars along Castro Street that have operated for decades. The neighborhood is a living history of the LGBTQ+ rights movement in the US.

**Chinatown**

Grant Avenue and the surrounding blocks — the oldest Chinatown in the US, established during the Gold Rush era when Chinese immigrants arrived in large numbers. Dense, active, and genuinely lived-in (not a tourist performance). The dim sum bakeries, the herbal medicine shops, and the produce markets coexist with tourist souvenir shops.

**The Mission (LGBTQ+, food, murals)**

Already covered in the food guide, but beyond the burritos: the Mission has the highest concentration of murals in San Francisco — Balmy Alley (an entire alley of painted garage doors) and the Women's Building mural on 18th Street are the reference points. The Latino community has deep roots here.

## Parks and Outdoors

**Golden Gate Park**

1,017 acres in the western part of the city — larger than Central Park, stretching from the Panhandle near Haight-Ashbury to the Pacific Ocean. Key areas:
- **Japanese Tea Garden**: $10 entry, opens at 9am. One of the oldest Japanese gardens in the US (1894). Best visited early before crowds.
- **de Young Museum**: Art museum with a strong American art collection and an observation tower with park and city views. $25; free first Tuesday of the month.
- **Stow Lake**: A lake with a boathouse and rowboat rentals ($22/hour). Circumference trail around the lake.

**Lands End and the Coastal Trail**

The northwestern corner of San Francisco — a coastal trail through cypress forest above the rocky Pacific shore, with views of the Golden Gate and the Marin Headlands. Free, accessible by Muni. The Louis' Restaurant at the trailhead (902 Point Lobos Avenue) has been serving breakfast on the cliff since 1937.

**Crissy Field**

A restored tidal marsh and beach on the northern waterfront — the best close-up view of the Golden Gate Bridge from inside the city. The walk from the Ferry Building west along the waterfront to Crissy Field is 3.5 miles; return via the Presidio.

- Day 2: Golden Gate Park + de Young + Haight-Ashbury + Mission dinner

## Practical Notes

**Hills**: San Francisco's hills are real — the steepest streets (Filbert Street near Coit Tower, Duboce Avenue) require effort. The main tourist corridors (Market Street, the Embarcadero, the Mission) are flat or gently sloped. Wear comfortable walking shoes.

**Weather**: 18–22°C in July with fog. Carry a light jacket; the temperature drops in the evenings regardless of how warm the afternoon was.

**Safety**: San Francisco has concentrated poverty and a visible street homeless population, particularly in the Tenderloin neighborhood (between Union Square and Civic Center). This neighborhood is not dangerous for visitors who are aware of their surroundings, but it is confronting. The areas most visited by tourists — the waterfront, the Mission, SOMA, North Beach, Chinatown — are generally safe.

**Getting around internally**: Muni is functional. Rideshare is easy. The hills make cycling moderate to difficult on certain routes; the Embarcadero and Golden Gate Park are excellent flat cycling options.
