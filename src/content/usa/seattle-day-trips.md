Seattle is surrounded by one of the most dramatic natural environments of any American city. Two national parks (Mount Rainier and Olympic) are within 90 minutes. The Cascade Range creates alpine scenery to the east; the Olympic Mountains define the horizon to the west across Puget Sound. The islands of the San Juan archipelago are accessible by ferry. For World Cup visitors with non-match days, the outdoor options are extraordinary.

---

## Mount Rainier

**Distance**: 90 miles southeast via SR-7 or US-12  
**Driving time**: 1.5–2 hours to the Paradise area  
**Best for**: Full day mountain/nature

Mount Rainier (4,392 meters) is an active stratovolcano — the most glaciated peak in the contiguous United States, with 26 named glaciers. It is visible from Seattle on clear days, dominating the southern horizon.

**Paradise (visitor center and trails)**: At 1,640 meters elevation, the Paradise area has the most accessible alpine terrain in the park. The Paradise Visitor Center (open daily in summer) has exhibits on the mountain's geology and ecology. From the parking area:

- **Skyline Trail** (5.5 miles loop): The most complete overview of the alpine meadows, permanent snowfields (at elevation), and Rainier's glaciers. Elevation gain: 450 meters. Allow 3–4 hours.
- **Myrtle Falls** (1.6 miles round-trip): An accessible waterfall trail with summit views. A good option for visitors with limited hiking time.

**July conditions**: Paradise is typically snow-free by late June. The wildflower meadows are at peak bloom July–August — lupine, paintbrush, and avalanche lily. The mountain is clear more than half the summer days.

**Sunrise (7,000 ft elevation)**: On the northeast side of the mountain — the highest road point in the park. More dramatic views of the summit and the Emmons Glacier. 2.5 hours from Seattle via US-2.

**Entry fee**: $35/vehicle, valid 7 days. Book Paradise parking in advance at recreation.gov — it fills by 9am on clear summer days.

**Without a car**: Rainier Shuttle runs from Ashford (an Amtrak/bus-accessible point) into the park. More complicated; the car option is strongly preferred.

## The Olympic Peninsula

**Distance**: 50 miles west via Highway 101 (ferry to Kingston or Hood Canal Bridge)  
**Driving time (with ferry)**: 2.5–3 hours to the park's Hoh Rain Forest  
**Best for**: Full day wilderness/coast

The Olympic Peninsula is one of the most ecologically diverse landscapes in North America — temperate rain forests on the western slope (the Hoh Rain Forest receives 140+ inches of rain annually), alpine terrain in the Olympic Mountains, and a wild Pacific coastline.

**Getting there**: Washington State Ferries from Edmonds (25 miles north of Seattle) to Kingston, then west across the peninsula. Or the Hood Canal Bridge on US-101. Car required.

**Hoh Rain Forest**: The most spectacular temperate rain forest accessible from Seattle. Trees 250+ feet tall draped in club moss — an environment that looks unlike any other landscape in the US. The Hall of Mosses trail (0.8 miles loop) is the essential walk; the Hoh River Trail extends to alpine country.

**Ruby Beach**: A beach on the Olympic coast with sea stacks (rock formations rising from the ocean), tide pools, and driftwood. 3 hours from Seattle. The Pacific here is cold and not for swimming, but the coastal scenery is dramatic.

**Sol Duc Hot Springs**: Natural hot springs in the western part of the park — soaking pools ranging 100–104°F in a forest setting. $25/person access fee. A combination of Olympic Peninsula driving and hot spring soaking is a genuinely distinctive Pacific Northwest day.

## Snoqualmie Falls and the Snoqualmie Valley

**Distance**: 30 miles east via I-90  
**Driving time**: 40–50 minutes  
**Best for**: Half-day nature/wine

Snoqualmie Falls is a 268-foot waterfall (taller than Niagara) on the Snoqualmie River in the foothills of the Cascades — the most visited natural attraction in Washington state. The viewing platform at the top gives the full width of the falls; a trail descends to the base.

The **Salish Lodge & Spa** (6501 Railroad Avenue SE) sits on the cliff above the falls — the lodge used as the exterior setting for the Twin Peaks TV series. Brunch on the deck with the falls audible below: $45–65 per person.

The **Snoqualmie Valley** below the falls has several Washington wine tasting rooms open on weekends — a modest but pleasant wine region distinct from the main Walla Walla production area.

## San Juan Islands

**Distance**: 80 miles northwest via ferry from Anacortes  
**Ferry time from Anacortes**: 1–3 hours depending on destination island  
**Best for**: Full day or overnight

The San Juan Islands are an archipelago of 172 islands in the northern Puget Sound — rocky, forested, with whale watching (orca, humpback), kayaking, and a pastoral island character.

**San Juan Island** (Friday Harbor): The most visited, with ferry access, whale watching tours ($75–100), and the Friday Harbor restaurants. The Lime Kiln Point State Park on the western shore has the best land-based orca viewing in the US.

**Orcas Island**: The largest and most rugged — Moran State Park with Mt. Constitution (the highest point in the islands, 2,400 feet), and Cascade Lake. More hiking-oriented than San Juan Island.

**Getting there**: Drive to Anacortes (85 miles north of Seattle via I-5) or take Airporter Shuttle, then Washington State Ferries. The combined journey is long for a day trip; the islands are better as an overnight.

## Whidbey Island

**Distance**: 30 miles north of Seattle (ferry from Mukilteo)  
**Ferry time**: 20 minutes  
**Best for**: Half-day drive and beaches

Whidbey Island is accessible by ferry from Mukilteo (30 minutes from Seattle on I-5, then 20-minute ferry to Clinton). The island is 45 miles long — farmland, beaches, the Ebey's Landing National Historical Reserve (some of the oldest farmland in the Pacific Northwest), and the small towns of Langley and Coupeville.

Practical and accessible for a half-day without the commitment of the Olympic Peninsula drive.
