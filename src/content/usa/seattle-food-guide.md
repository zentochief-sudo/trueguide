Seattle's food identity is defined by geography: the Pacific Ocean, the Puget Sound, the rivers of the Olympic Peninsula and the Cascades, and the farmland of the Skagit Valley all contribute to a food supply that has made this one of the best seafood cities in the United States. The Vietnamese, Filipino, and Japanese communities have added depth to a food culture that is also shaped by the farm-to-table movement that took particularly strong root in the Pacific Northwest.

---

## Pike Place Market

**Pike Place Market** (1 Pike Place) is the starting point for any Seattle food visit. Operating since 1907, it is the oldest continuously operated farmers market in the US — and an active, functional market (not a tourist re-creation) where local farmers, fishmongers, and small food producers sell daily.

**What to eat and buy**:
- **Pike Place Fish Company**: The fish-throwing fishmongers that became the market's most-photographed feature. Fresh Pacific salmon, halibut, Dungeness crab, and oysters. Buy cleaned whole salmon to cook ($25–35/lb); or have them ship it packed for your flight.
- **Beecher's Handmade Cheese**: A cheesemaker inside the market, visible through glass, producing fresh curds and their flagship Flagship cheddar. The mac and cheese ($10) made with the Flagship is the market's best quick meal.
- **Pike Place Chowder** (1530 Post Alley): The best clam chowder in Seattle — a New England-style white clam chowder in a bread bowl, won multiple national chowder competitions. $10–12. Expect a line at lunch.
- **The Crumpet Shop** (1503 1st Avenue): British-style crumpets made fresh, served with toppings. A Seattle breakfast institution.
- **Rachel the Pig** (the market's bronze pig sculpture): The donation pig at the market entrance. Not food, but the photo landmark.

## Pacific Seafood

Seattle's claim on Pacific seafood is genuine — the proximity to Alaskan fisheries, the daily ferry of fish through Seattle's port, and the tradition of commercial fishing has created a fresh seafood infrastructure that most American cities cannot match.

**Pacific salmon**: Five species — King (Chinook), Silver (Coho), Sockeye, Pink, and Chum. King salmon is the largest, most prized, with high fat content. Sockeye is the most flavorful for grilling. July is mid-season for several species. Order "wild-caught Pacific salmon" specifically — farmed Atlantic salmon is a different product.

**Dungeness crab**: The Pacific Coast's signature shellfish — larger than East Coast blue crab, with sweet white meat. Season peaks November–June but availability continues through summer. At Pike Place: $30–45 for a whole cleaned crab.

**Pacific oysters**: Hood Canal oysters (a Puget Sound sub-inlet) and Westcott Bay oysters (San Juan Islands) are the local varieties. Tasted raw with mignonette or grilled with garlic butter. $3–4.50/each.

**Key seafood restaurants**:

**The Walrus and the Carpenter** (4743 Ballard Ave NW, Ballard): A small oyster bar with a changing seasonal menu — the best raw oyster selection in Seattle, with Pacific varieties served simply. Lines form before opening; the bar fills quickly. Dinner: $55–70 per person. No reservations; arrive early.

**Taylor Shellfish Farms** (multiple locations including Capitol Hill): The Puget Sound's largest shellfish farm has retail and restaurant operations. Oysters, clams, and geoduck (the massive Pacific clam with a flavor unlike anything on the East Coast). A dozen oysters at the bar: $24–36.

**Ivar's Acres of Clams** (1001 Alaskan Way, Pier 54): A Seattle institution since 1938 — fried clam strips, fish and chips, clam chowder on the waterfront. Not the city's most innovative restaurant, but genuinely good value and historically embedded. Fish and chips: $14–18.

**Canlis** (2576 Aurora Avenue N): Seattle's flagship fine dining restaurant, operating since 1950 with Lake Union views. Pacific seafood treated with full technique — Dungeness crab, halibut, wild salmon in the contemporary PNW style. Dinner: $90–130 per person. Reservations required well in advance.

## Asian Food

Seattle's significant Japanese, Vietnamese, Filipino, Chinese, and Korean communities have shaped the food culture over decades.

**Pho Bắc** (1314 S. Jackson Street, International District): The International District (Seattle's combined Chinatown/Japantown/Little Saigon) has the city's best Vietnamese pho — this location has been operating since 1982. Pho: $14–16.

**Maneki** (304 6th Ave S): The oldest Japanese restaurant in Seattle, operating since 1904. Izakaya format — small plates, sake, and the specific atmosphere of a restaurant that has operated continuously for over a century.

**Musashi's** (1400 N 45th Street, Wallingford): Counter-service Japanese food — teriyaki, tonkatsu, and the Seattle teriyaki bowl (a Seattle-specific format of rice + grilled teriyaki meat + steamed vegetables that is meaningfully different from Japanese teriyaki). $12–15.

**Ba Bar** (550 12th Avenue, Capitol Hill): Vietnamese bánh mì and pho in a comfortable Capitol Hill space — the most accessible Vietnamese option for visitors staying in the main neighborhoods. $14–18.

## Farm-to-Table Pacific Northwest

**Sitka & Spruce** (1531 Melrose Avenue, Capitol Hill): The most influential PNW cooking in Seattle — small plates built around the seasonal ingredients of the Skagit Valley and the Pacific, with minimal intervention. The restaurant has influenced a generation of Seattle cooks. Dinner: $60–80 per person.

**The Whale Wins** (3506 Stone Way N, Fremont): Wood-oven cooking from the same chef group as Sitka & Spruce — vegetables, fish, and meats cooked over live fire with PNW flavors. More accessible than Sitka. Dinner: $50–65 per person.

**Canteen** (1338 E. Thomas Street, Capitol Hill): A small neighborhood restaurant with a locavore menu that changes with the week's Skagit Valley delivery. The kind of place that defines neighborhood cooking at its best.

## The Original Starbucks

**1912 Pike Place** (the original Starbucks location): The first store opened here in 1971. The current operation is essentially a tourist experience — long lines, the original logo merchandise — but the store itself is architecturally unchanged and the context (inside Pike Place Market) is authentic. Worth a quick visit as context for understanding Seattle's role in creating the contemporary coffee shop format, even if you prefer independent roasters.
