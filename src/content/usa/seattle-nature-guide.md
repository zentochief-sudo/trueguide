Seattle's outdoor access within the city limits is exceptional for an urban environment — a 534-acre forest park on the sound, a lake inside the city connected to the ocean, and a network of trails that make it possible to hike, kayak, or cycle without leaving Seattle. July's long days (sunset at 9pm) and typically dry weather create the best conditions of the year for outdoor activity.

---

## Discovery Park

**Access**: Wedgwood/Magnolia neighborhood; bus routes 19, 24, 33 from downtown, or rideshare  
**Size**: 534 acres — the largest park in Seattle  
**Character**: Old-growth forest, bluffs above Puget Sound, coastal trails

Discovery Park occupies a former military base (Fort Lawton) on a bluff above Puget Sound in the Magnolia neighborhood. It has the most complete urban wilderness experience in Seattle — genuine old-growth Douglas fir forest, meadows, wetlands, sea bluffs, and a beach.

**The Loop Trail** (2.8 miles): The primary circuit through the park, through forest and meadow with views of the Olympic Mountains across the Sound on clear days.

**South Meadow and West Point Beach**: A walk through the meadow to the lighthouse at West Point — the only spot in Seattle where you stand at water level with the full Sound and the Olympics in view. The beach is rocky (Pacific Northwest beaches are generally not sand beaches), but the light in the evening is exceptional.

**The Daybreak Star Indian Cultural Center** (inside the park): A Native American cultural center run by the United Indians of All Tribes Foundation. The building and its contemporary Native art collection are open to the public.

**Free entry**. The park is open daily; the parking lot fills on weekend afternoons — arrive before 10am or use transit.

## Lake Union and Kayaking

**Access**: Eastlake neighborhood (10 minutes from downtown by bus or rideshare)  
**Character**: Urban lake with houseboats, seaplanes, kayaking

Lake Union is a freshwater lake in the middle of Seattle, connected to Puget Sound via the Chittenden Locks in Ballard. The lake is framed by houseboats (520 registered houseboats in Seattle — more than anywhere in the US), the Gas Works Park on the north shore, and the Eastlake neighborhood on the east.

**Kayaking from Eastlake**: Northwest Outdoor Center (2100 Westlake Avenue N) rents single and tandem kayaks — $20–28/hour. Paddling across Lake Union to the houseboat communities, around the marinas, and under the seaplane flight paths is the most engaging urban outdoor activity in Seattle. July evening paddles with the 9pm sunset over Queen Anne Hill are specifically worth the effort.

**Gas Works Park**: A decommissioned gasification plant converted into a park — the industrial ruins left standing as an architectural landmark. The hill at the north end of the park has the best view of the Seattle skyline, Lake Union, and Mount Rainier (on clear days). Popular for summer evening picnics.

## Burke-Gilman Trail

**Length**: 27 miles (Burke-Gilman portion); 50+ miles total (Sammamish River Trail extension)  
**Access**: Multiple entry points; most accessible from Fremont or the University District  
**Character**: Urban cycling trail along former rail corridor

The Burke-Gilman Trail is Seattle's primary cycling and running corridor — a paved trail that runs from Ballard through Fremont, past the University of Washington, along Lake Washington, and continues to Redmond. Flat and well-maintained, it is the most practical cycling route in the city.

**Bike rentals**: Lime and Wheels bikes (dockless rentals) are available throughout the trail corridor. $1 unlock + $0.35/minute. A 2-hour ride from Fremont to the Bothell trailhead and back: approximately $25–30.

## Green Lake

**Access**: Phinney Ridge neighborhood; buses 5, 16 from downtown  
**Character**: Urban lake with 2.8-mile perimeter trail

Green Lake is a shallow urban lake in north Seattle surrounded by a 2.8-mile paved trail. It is the most heavily used park in Seattle — on summer evenings, the perimeter trail has continuous foot and cycle traffic. Less wilderness than Discovery Park, more social — the trail is where Seattle runs and rolls.

The **Green Lake Small Craft Center** (7312 W Green Lake Drive N) rents rowboats, paddleboats, and kayaks: $15–20/hour.

## Chittenden Locks (Ballard Locks)

**Access**: Ballard neighborhood; bus 44 from downtown  
**Character**: Engineering landmark, salmon viewing

The Hiram M. Chittenden Locks connect the freshwater Lake Washington Ship Canal to the saltwater Puget Sound. Boats raise and lower between the two water levels through a lock system; a fish ladder alongside the main lock allows salmon to migrate between salt and fresh water.

**Fish ladder viewing**: July–August is the peak salmon migration season. Pacific salmon (Sockeye, Coho, and Chinook depending on species timing) pass through the fish ladder in numbers visible from the underwater viewing windows. The combination of watching 15-pound salmon fight upstream and the boats locking through on their way to sea is a specifically Pacific Northwest juxtaposition.

**Free entry**. Open daily 7am–9pm. The adjacent Carl S. English Jr. Botanical Garden has well-maintained grounds.

## Alki Beach

**Access**: West Seattle, water taxi from Pier 50 or drive via 1st Avenue Bridge  
**Character**: Urban beach with Olympic Mountain views

Alki Beach is a 2.5-mile beach in West Seattle — Seattle's closest ocean beach, with views of the Olympic Mountains across the Sound. Water temperatures are cold (14–16°C in July), which limits swimming, but the beach walk and the evening light on the mountains are worth the 20-minute water taxi from downtown.

The water taxi (King County) runs from Pier 50 to the Seacrest Dock in West Seattle — $5.75 each way, 10 minutes. Return the same way or via bus 50.
