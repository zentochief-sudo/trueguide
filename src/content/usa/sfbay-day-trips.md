San Francisco is surrounded by outstanding natural landscapes that most American cities do not have within a half-day's reach. The Pacific coastline, old-growth redwood forests, the Big Sur coast, and Yosemite Valley are all accessible as day trips — though Yosemite is at the extreme end of what "day trip" means. This guide gives realistic transport times and honest assessments of what's worth the travel.

---

## Muir Woods and the Marin Headlands

**Distance**: 17 miles north across the Golden Gate Bridge  
**Driving time**: 30–40 minutes  
**Best for**: Half-day nature / redwoods

Muir Woods National Monument is a stand of old-growth coast redwood trees — trees that grow 60–100 meters tall and live 500–1,200 years. The access from a major city is exceptional; the forest floor experience (the light filtering through the canopy, the scale of the trunks, the silence) is unlike anything in the urban US.

**Practical notes**:
- **Reservations required**: Muir Woods operates a timed-entry reservation system (recreation.gov). Book 2–3 weeks in advance during summer. Day-of tickets are rarely available.
- **Car**: US-101 north, exit at Highway 1 Stinson Beach. The parking lot requires a reservation ($8/vehicle). The road is a single-lane canyon approach — arrive before 10am.
- **Without a car**: The **Muir Woods Shuttle** from the Sausalito Ferry Terminal operates on weekends and holidays. Ferry from SF to Sausalito ($14), then shuttle ($3.50). Journey: 45–60 minutes from the ferry terminal.
- **Fee**: $15/person park entry.

**Combine with**: A walk into the Marin Headlands for the Golden Gate Bridge views (Battery Spencer), then return via the ferry from Sausalito with lunch at a waterfront restaurant.

## Sausalito

**Distance**: 1 mile across the Bay from SF (by ferry)  
**Ferry time**: 30 minutes  
**Best for**: Half-day waterfront

Sausalito is a small waterfront town on the Marin side of the Bay, directly across from San Francisco. The view looking back at SF and the Bay Bridge from the Sausalito waterfront is one of the best Bay views available.

**What to do**: Walk the waterfront promenade, lunch at **Fish.** (sustainable seafood on the water — the best quick seafood lunch in the Bay Area), browse the galleries on Bridgeway Avenue.

**The ferry**: Golden Gate Ferry from the Ferry Building ($14 each way). The return trip at sunset with SF silhouetted against the western light is the ideal.

## Monterey and Carmel

**Distance**: 115 miles south via US-101 or Highway 1  
**Driving time**: 2–2.5 hours via 101; 2.5–3 hours via the coastal Highway 1  
**Best for**: Full day

Monterey is a coastal town with the most accessible and impressive marine life viewing on the California coast. The **Monterey Bay Aquarium** (886 Cannery Row) — built on the site of a former sardine cannery — has a 35-foot tall kelp forest tank, sea otters, and a pelagic open-sea exhibit. It is one of the best aquariums in the world. Admission: $55.

**17-Mile Drive**: A private toll road through the Pebble Beach golf courses and Carmel-by-the-Sea, with the most dramatic coastline in the Bay Area day trip range. $12.25 toll per vehicle. The Lone Cypress — a single tree on a granite point photographed more than almost any tree in the world — is on this drive.

**Carmel-by-the-Sea**: A small town with galleries, wine tasting rooms, and the beach. The town has no traffic lights, no street addresses (residents pick up mail at the post office), and an ordinance against high heels without permits (a historical curiosity). The beach is white sand and cold Pacific — not swimming weather but beautiful.

**Without a car**: Amtrak Thruway bus from San Francisco to Monterey (2.5–3 hours, $30–40). Return buses run on a schedule — verify times before going.

## Big Sur Coast

**Distance**: 150 miles south via Highway 1  
**Driving time**: 3–3.5 hours  
**Best for**: Full day drive

Highway 1 through Big Sur is the most dramatic coastal road in the US — 90 miles of cliffs above the Pacific, redwood forests in the river canyons, no significant towns for hours. Not a destination with a specific attraction but an experience of the California coast in its most extreme form.

**Key points**:
- **Bixby Creek Bridge**: The iconic single-arch concrete bridge photographed on every California coast feature
- **McWay Falls** (Julia Pfeiffer Burns State Park): An 80-foot waterfall falling onto a beach — accessible via a 0.6-mile trail
- **Ventana Big Sur**: A resort and spa at the heart of Big Sur. Lunch ($40–60) at the Corazón restaurant is the practical midpoint stop.

**Practical note**: Highway 1 through Big Sur is a two-lane coastal road. Portions close periodically due to landslides — check Caltrans for current road conditions before departing.

## Yosemite National Park

**Distance**: 195 miles east via I-580 and Highway 120  
**Driving time**: 3.5–4 hours to Yosemite Valley  
**Best for**: Full day (early start required) or overnight

Yosemite is reachable as a very long day trip — but the early departure (leave SF by 6am) and late return (arrive back at midnight) make it better suited as an overnight.

**July conditions**: Summer is peak season. The Tioga Pass Road (the high-elevation east-west route) is open. The valley is warm (30–35°C) and extremely crowded. **Timed-entry reservations** are required for Yosemite Valley between 5am and 4pm on peak days — book at recreation.gov months in advance.

**What to see**: Yosemite Valley floor has El Capitan (the 900-meter granite monolith), Yosemite Falls (the highest waterfall in North America at 739 meters), Half Dome, Bridalveil Fall. The Valley Loop Drive is the vehicle-accessible overview; the Mist Trail to Vernal Fall (3.6 miles roundtrip) is the most accessible hike.

**Without a car**: YARTS (Yosemite Area Regional Transportation System) buses run from Merced, Fresno, and Mammoth Lakes. From SF: BART/Amtrak to Merced, then YARTS to the Valley. Journey: 5–6 hours each way. Feasible only as an overnight.

## Half Moon Bay (Pacific Coast)

**Distance**: 28 miles south via Highway 1  
**Driving time**: 40–55 minutes  
**Best for**: Half-day coast

Half Moon Bay is the closest Pacific Coast beach to San Francisco — rockier and colder than Southern California beaches (water temperature 14–16°C in July), with a dramatic coastline. The Ritz-Carlton Half Moon Bay (the only oceanfront resort in the Bay Area) occupies the bluff above the water; lunch there is $45–65 per person with ocean views.

**Pescadero** (15 miles further south): A small agricultural town with Duarte's Tavern (202 Stage Road) — a family restaurant operating since 1894 with the best artichoke soup on the coast ($9). Pescadero Marsh is a coastal wetland with bird life.
