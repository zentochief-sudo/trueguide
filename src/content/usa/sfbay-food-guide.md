San Francisco has one of the strongest restaurant cultures in the United States — a combination of proximity to exceptional agricultural products (Central Valley vegetables, Pacific seafood, Napa/Sonoma wine), a concentration of culinary talent, and a food culture that has been serious for decades longer than most American cities. The challenge for visitors is navigating a city that has neighborhoods with very different price points and cuisines.

---

## The Mission Taqueria Circuit

The single best food value in San Francisco is a burrito in the Mission District. The Mission-style burrito — a flour tortilla wrapped around rice, beans, meat, salsa, sour cream, and guacamole — originated in this neighborhood and remains its defining food.

**La Taqueria** (2889 Mission Street): The James Beard Award-winning taqueria, frequently cited as the best burrito in the country. The carnitas burrito without rice (the rice adds filler without flavor) is the reference order. No-frills space, fast service, $11–13. Lines at lunch.

**Taqueria Cancún** (2288 Mission Street): A valid alternative with a broader menu. The carne asada is excellent; the salsa bar allows customization. $9–12.

**El Farolito** (2779 Mission Street): Open until 2:30am, making it the post-bar option. The adobada (marinated pork) tacos at midnight are worth the trip independently. $9–12.

## Dim Sum

San Francisco's Chinatown is the oldest in the US (established 1848), and the dim sum culture in the wider city — particularly in the Richmond District — is exceptional.

**Yank Sing** (101 Spear Street): Upscale dim sum in SOMA, popular with the Financial District lunch crowd. The dumplings are technically excellent, the setting is polished, and it's the most accessible entry point for visitors unfamiliar with the format. Lunch for two: $55–75. Weekends have a wait.

**Hong Kong Lounge II** (3300 Geary Boulevard): The Richmond District's most acclaimed dim sum — more casual and locally-oriented than Yank Sing, with a menu that runs longer. The har gow and the egg custard tarts are the reference items. Lunch: $20–30 per person.

**Good Mong Kok Bakery** (1039 Stockton Street, Chinatown): Dim sum in a take-away format — plates of shumai, turnip cakes, and roast pork buns at $3–5. For eating while walking through Chinatown at 9am.

## The Ferry Building

The Ferry Building Marketplace (1 Ferry Building) is a farmers market inside the historic 1898 ferry terminal building on the Embarcadero. It operates a full market on Tuesdays, Thursdays, and Saturdays; the indoor food hall runs daily.

**Saturday Market** (8am–2pm): The best food market experience in the Bay Area. Cowgirl Creamery (artisan cheeses), Acme Bread Company (the reference sourdough in a city known for sourdough), Blue Bottle Coffee (before it became a chain), local oysters, seasonal produce from Central Valley farms.

**Hog Island Oyster Company** (ferry building): Pacific oysters from farms in Tomales Bay, shucked to order with mignonette. $20–28 for a half dozen. The best quick food experience on the waterfront.

**The food hall vendors**: Boccalone (Italian cured meats), McEvoy Ranch olive oil, Recchiuti Confections. The Ferry Building is the one place in San Francisco where eating and shopping for food occupy the same beautiful historic space.

## Top Restaurants

**Zuni Café** (1658 Market Street): The San Francisco institution — a brick-walled restaurant with a wood-burning oven that has been operating since 1979. The roast chicken for two (with bread salad) is the dish that defines San Francisco cooking more than any other — order it 45 minutes in advance. Dinner for two: $90–120.

**State Bird Provisions** (1529 Fillmore Street): A SF original format — dim sum service of California small plates, pushed on carts through the room. The crispy pig head with a fried quail egg is the kind of dish that wins James Beard Awards (it did). Reservations months in advance; bar seating walk-in available.

**Che Fico** (838 Divisadero): Italian-California in the Divisadero corridor — the wood-oven pizza and the pasta program reflect northern California's relationship with northern Italian cooking. Dinner: $50–70 per person.

**Liholiho Yacht Club** (871 Sutter Street): Hawaiian-California cooking — the cultural intersection of the Bay Area's large Hawaiian and Asian populations with California produce. The tuna dip and the oxtail fried rice are the signatures. Dinner: $55–75 per person.

## Coffee

San Francisco is the city where the third-wave coffee movement developed, and the density of serious coffee operations remains high.

**Sightglass Coffee** (7th Street, SOMA): A large café in a converted light industrial space — the SOMA location has the best combination of coffee program and space. Single origin pour-overs and espresso drinks from a serious sourcing program.

**Ritual Coffee Roasters** (multiple locations): The roaster that preceded Blue Bottle in establishing the SF coffee aesthetic. The Valencia Street location in the Mission is the flagship.

**Tartine Bakery** (600 Guerrero Street, Mission): The most acclaimed bakery in SF — the country bread ($14), the morning buns (available 8am, frequently sold out by 9:30am), and the croissants. The evening bread line forms at 5pm for loaves pulled from the oven at 6pm.

## Seafood

Pacific seafood is one of the Bay Area's defining culinary strengths.

**Dungeness crab** (seasonal, peaks November–June): The Bay Area's signature shellfish. If any overlap exists with the World Cup dates, the crab season may offer fresh-caught crab at the Ferry Building or Fish. (In July, the Dungeness season is transitional — availability varies.)

**Fish.** (350 Harbor Drive, Sausalito): A sustainable seafood restaurant on the Sausalito waterfront — 30 minutes by ferry from San Francisco. Fish tacos, clam chowder, and oysters in one of the most beautiful settings in the Bay. Plan as a half-day from SF combining the ferry and lunch.

**Swan Oyster Depot** (1517 Polk Street): A lunch counter that has operated since 1912 — fresh oysters, clam chowder, crab salad, shrimp cocktail. Cash only, seats about 18, line forms before opening. A quintessential SF experience. Open through early afternoon.

## Practical Notes

**Cost**: San Francisco restaurant prices are high — $18–22 for a breakfast, $35–55 per person at casual dinner, $75–120 per person at a nicer restaurant. The Mission taquerias and Chinatown dim sum are the genuine value options.

**Reservations**: State Bird Provisions, Zuni, and several other SF restaurants book out weeks to months in advance. Walk-in bar seating at most restaurants is available if reservations are gone.

**Sourdough**: San Francisco sourdough has a specific tanginess from the local Lactobacillus sanfranciscensis bacteria — it doesn't exist in the same way outside the Bay Area. Boudin Bakery at Fisherman's Wharf is the tourist version (fine); Tartine and Acme are the serious version.
