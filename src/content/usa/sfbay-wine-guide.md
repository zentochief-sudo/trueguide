California wine country is one of the most compelling reasons to spend extra days in the SF Bay Area around World Cup matches. Napa Valley and Sonoma County are both within 60–90 minutes of [San Francisco](/united-states/san-francisco-guide), both at peak season in July (the vines are full, temperatures are warm, harvest is approaching), and both offer experiences that don't exist anywhere else in proximity to a World Cup venue.

The two regions have different characters: Napa is more formal, more expensive, and more focused on Cabernet Sauvignon; Sonoma is larger, more casual, more diverse in varietals, and more accessible for visitors without wine expertise. Both are worth the trip.

---

## Napa Valley

**What it is**: A 30-mile valley north of San Francisco between two mountain ranges. The Napa River runs through the valley floor; the terroir is shaped by the fog that rolls in from the Bay in the mornings and burns off by noon. The valley grows primarily Cabernet Sauvignon (the dominant varietal in the Napa appellation), Chardonnay, Merlot, and Sauvignon Blanc.

**Why it matters**: Napa achieved international recognition at the 1976 Paris Tasting (the "Judgment of Paris"), when California wines beat French Burgundies and Bordeaux in a blind tasting by French judges — an event that established California winemaking on the world stage. The story is told in the film "Bottle Shock" (2008) and at the Culinary Institute of America's Copia museum in Napa city.

## Napa Wineries Worth Visiting

**Domaine Carneros** (1240 Duhig Road, Napa): A château-style winery in the Carneros appellation making Champagne-method sparkling wines — the Carneros terroir (cool, foggy) is ideal for sparkling. The terrace tasting with a valley view is the most civilized way to start a wine day. Tasting: $40–60.

**Stag's Leap Wine Cellars** (5766 Silverado Trail): The winery whose 1973 S.L.V. Cabernet won the Paris Tasting. Significant historical resonance; excellent Cabernet program. Tasting by appointment: $75–100.

**Beringer Vineyards** (2000 Main Street, St. Helena): The oldest continuously operating winery in Napa (since 1876), survived Prohibition by making sacramental wine. The Rhine House is a Victorian mansion; the caves cut into the limestone hillside predate electricity. One of the few wineries where the history is as interesting as the wine. Tasting: $40–75.

**Cakebread Cellars** (8300 St. Helena Highway): A family winery known for consistent, elegant Chardonnay and Cabernet. The setting in the vineyard with the Mayacamas Mountains behind it is archetypically Napa. Reservation required; tasting: $50–80.

**Oxbow Public Market** (610 and 644 First Street, Napa): Not a winery but an essential stop — a food hall in the Napa city waterfront with local producers, cheese, charcuterie, and tasting bars. A good introduction to Napa food culture before heading into the valley.

## Sonoma County

Sonoma is larger and more diverse than Napa — 17 distinct American Viticultural Areas (AVAs) producing everything from Pinot Noir in the Sonoma Coast appellation (cooled by the Pacific) to Zinfandel in the Dry Creek Valley (warm and dry). The wine culture is more casual; some Sonoma wineries still operate in the working-farm mode, with chickens and vegetable gardens alongside the vines.

**Healdsburg** is the base town for Sonoma wine visits — a small town with a central plaza lined with wine tasting rooms, excellent restaurants, and walkable access to multiple wineries. It's 70 miles north of San Francisco via US-101.

---

## Sonoma Wineries Worth Visiting

**Ridge Vineyards** (650 Lytton Springs Road, Healdsburg): Zinfandel specialists in the Dry Creek Valley — the Lytton Springs Zinfandel is a benchmark wine, distinctive and food-friendly. The winery is unpretentious and the tasting room is converted farm buildings. Tasting: $30–40.

**Dry Creek Vineyard** (3770 Lambert Bridge Road, Healdsburg): Old-vine Zinfandel in the Dry Creek Valley. One of the more approachable wineries with a picnic-on-the-lawn format. Tasting: $25–35.

**Jordan Winery** (1474 Alexander Valley Road, Healdsburg): An estate producing Cabernet Sauvignon and Chardonnay in the Alexander Valley — more formal than most Sonoma wineries, known for hospitality as much as the wine. The library tasting includes older vintages. Reservation required; tasting: $50–100.

**Chalk Hill Estate** (10300 Chalk Hill Road, Healdsburg): A large estate winery in a sub-appellation named after the white volcanic ash soil. The Chardonnay from Chalk Hill is the reference varietal. Tasting: $50–75.

## Getting to Wine Country

**Without a car (Napa)**:
- **BART + VINE bus**: BART from San Francisco to the El Cerrito del Norte or Richmond station, then the VINE Bus 29 to Napa. Journey: 90–120 minutes total. Cost: ~$12. The bus runs on a schedule; verify return times before departing.
- **SMART train to Sonoma/Petaluma**: SMART commuter rail from San Rafael (BART connection at San Rafael) runs to Petaluma and Santa Rosa. Less useful for specific winery access.
- **Organized tour**: Bus tours from San Francisco depart from Fisherman's Wharf or Union Square. Half-day: $80–100; full day with tastings: $120–160. The most convenient option for non-drivers.

**With a car**:
- SF to Napa: US-101 north to Novato, then Highway 37 east to Napa. 60–75 minutes.
- SF to Healdsburg: US-101 north to Healdsburg exit. 75–90 minutes.
- **Sober driver note**: Wine tasting is not compatible with driving; designate a driver or use a car service. Napa and Sonoma have DUI enforcement specifically during tourist season.

**Car services from San Francisco**: Multiple companies operate wine country tours with driver — $400–600 for a private full-day car with driver, split among 4 people becomes $100–150/person.

## July Wine Country Context

July is late spring/early summer in wine country terms. The vines are in full canopy, the grapes are developing but not yet harvested (harvest is August–October). The visual experience — vine rows in full leaf on a warm, clear day — is at its peak.

Napa in July: 30–35°C in the valley. Start tastings before noon; the afternoon heat makes outdoor winery visits uncomfortable. Sonoma's Healdsburg: similar temperatures. Both regions cool to 18–22°C by evening.

## Wine Buying

Most wineries ship within the US; international shipping of wine is regulated and often impractical. If you want to bring wine home:
- Carry-on: Open containers not permitted; sealed bottles in carry-on are allowed under US TSA rules (but subject to destination country's alcohol import limits).
- Checked luggage: Use wine bag protectors (available at most wineries) for padding. 2–3 bottles per traveler is typical.
- Shipping: FedEx and UPS ship wine within the US from most wineries; international shipping varies by country of destination.
