FIFA World Cup 2026 is the largest World Cup in history: 48 teams, 16 host cities across three countries, and 104 matches played from June 11 to July 19, 2026. For the first time, the tournament spans an entire continent — the United States, Mexico, and Canada are co-hosting, with matches from Vancouver to Miami, Mexico City to Boston.

This guide covers everything a traveling fan needs to know: how to choose your city, where to buy tickets, how the format works, and what each host city offers beyond the stadium.

---

## The format: what changed in 2026

The 2026 World Cup expanded from 32 to 48 teams. The new structure:

- **Group stage**: 16 groups of 3 teams each. Each team plays 2 matches. The top 2 teams from each group advance (32 teams), plus the 8 best third-place finishers (8 teams) → 32 total advance.
- **Round of 32**: The first knockout round in World Cup history. 32 teams become 16.
- **Round of 16**: 16 teams become 8.
- **Quarter-finals**: 8 teams become 4.
- **Semi-finals**: 4 teams become 2.
- **Third-place match and Final**: July 18–19 at MetLife Stadium, New Jersey.

The expanded format means more matches in total (104 vs. 64 in previous tournaments) and more cities active across a longer period. A fan who buys two group stage matches in the same city can see 4–6 different national teams in the space of a week.

## Which city should you choose?

### For the biggest matches: New York/New Jersey and Dallas
MetLife hosts the Final and a semi-final. AT&T in Dallas hosts a quarter-final and multiple high-stakes knockout matches. If you want maximum football intensity, these are the priority cities.

### For the best combination of football + city experience: Mexico City
The Azteca hosts the opening match — the most iconic moment of the entire tournament — plus a quarter-final. CDMX is the richest non-football destination in the host country roster: 22 million people, UNESCO-heritage cuisine, world-class museums, and neighborhoods you could spend weeks in. [Full guide: Mexico City World Cup 2026 →](/mexico/cdmx-world-cup-2026-guide)

### For weather and beach proximity: Miami and Los Angeles
Miami's Hard Rock Stadium and LA's SoFi both offer warm summer conditions with ocean access within 20–45 minutes. Both are Round of 16 venues. [Miami guide →](/united-states/miami-world-cup-2026-guide) | [LA guide →](/united-states/la-world-cup-2026-guide)

### For first-time USA visitors: New York City
MetLife and the New York City experience is the single best combination for international visitors who want football AND America's most iconic city. Two group stage matches plus a knockout round match in the same city is achievable. [NYC guide →](/united-states/nyc-world-cup-2026-guide)

### For culture and barbecue: Dallas and Kansas City
Dallas has the best barbecue in the US and Tex-Mex built over a century. Kansas City has world-class barbecue of a different tradition (slow-smoked brisket and burnt ends) and a surprisingly strong arts and jazz scene. [Dallas guide →](/united-states/dallas-world-cup-2026-guide)

## Tickets

Tickets are available through [FIFA+ Ticketing](https://tickets.fifa.com) — the official platform. See our [complete ticket buying guide →](/united-states/world-cup-2026-tickets-guide) for prices, categories, how to buy, and last-minute options.

**Ticket categories**:
- **Category 1**: Premium seating, best views, highest price
- **Category 2**: Mid-tier seating
- **Category 3**: Budget option, typically behind the goals or upper tiers
- **Hospitality**: Packages with catering, lounges, and premium amenities

**Prices by round** (approximate, USD):
- Group stage: $55–$160 (Cat 3–1)
- Round of 32/16: $100–$280
- Quarter-finals: $150–$450
- Semi-finals: $200–$600
- Final (MetLife): $400–$1,500+

## Fan Zones

FIFA Official Fan Zones are free entry public events held in each host city on match days. Each Fan Zone has large screens broadcasting matches live, food and drink vendors, sponsor activations, and live entertainment.

Locations are set up in city parks and public spaces near downtown areas — not at the stadiums themselves. Check [FIFA.com/en/fifafanfestival](https://www.fifa.com) for the confirmed location in each city.

**Why Fan Zones matter**: If you don't have a ticket to a specific match but want to be part of the atmosphere, Fan Zones are the answer. They attract tens of thousands of people and create the street-level energy that defines a World Cup host city during the tournament.

## Practical logistics

**Getting between cities**: The US host cities are spread across the country. Domestic flights are the practical option for covering multiple cities. Dallas, Houston, Atlanta, and Miami are connected by multiple daily flights. New York–Miami: 3 hours. Dallas–Los Angeles: 2.5 hours. If you plan to attend matches in 2–3 cities over the tournament, map your schedule against the flight distances.

**Accommodation surge pricing**: Hotel rates in host cities during match weeks are 40–120% above normal summer rates. Booking in advance — particularly for the quarter-final and semi-final weeks — is essential. Alternative options: short-term rentals (Airbnb, Vrbo), suburbs and adjacent cities (New Jersey for NYC, Anaheim for LA, Arlington for Dallas).

**Travel within host cities**: Each city guide covers transport in detail. The general principle: cities with good public transit (New York, Seattle, Atlanta via MARTA) are more efficient on match days. Cities built around cars (Dallas, Miami, Houston, LA) require more planning.

**Health and travel insurance**: Match-day security and crowded venues are low-risk but travel insurance remains relevant for medical emergencies, trip cancellations, and flight disruptions.
